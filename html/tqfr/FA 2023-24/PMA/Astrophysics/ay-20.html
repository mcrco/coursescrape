Comments

What would you tell a student thinking about taking this course in the future?

Astronomy is fun, so you should consider taking this course! Sets are reasonable and you get to go to Palomar Observatory and take data from the P60 telescope. However, the observing project was not very-well-structured, and was way more work than this 9 unit course indicates. Further, the exam format was changed several times and the final exam was a 4 hour in person closed book + note exam (minus a 1 page cheat sheet we could make ourselves, trying to distill 1000+ pages of the course textbook onto one singular page and having no idea what we were going to be asked or the level of depth needed to answer the question). The consensus was that the exam was very very difficult, and the hopes are that it will not make this class a major GPA tank for us all.
Overall interesting course, although the content goes very fast and Prof. Kasliwal expects you to have a lot of previous background knowledge. Read the textbook (BoB) and keep a page of the important formulas and concepts as you go through the course, because the final exam is a closed-note exam and you will need to make a final formula sheet which covers around 1,000 pages of content. Additionally, be prepared for the research project because it is very long and feels like a SURF project compressed into 2 weeks. I would not recommend taking the course if you have zero python experience because the research project requires a lot of coding. Stay engaged and go to lecture.
All I can say is don't take this class unless you are an astro major and have to. And if you are majoring in astrophysics, don't let this class kill your passion for the subject–take it with a grain of salt and try and find the parts of it that do excite you (it will be hard).
If you are not an ay major or minor, I would not recommend taking this course. During the term, the student is content with the appropriate workload but at the expense of understanding anything meaningful about the topic. As an introductory course, it fails to instill basic knowledge in the students, relying on memorization of formulas. As with most Caltech courses, the pacing of the course is the main reason why retention is impossible. However, unlike most physics courses (ph1abc and ph12a) it does not seek to justify derivations as thoroughly and lacks the character of investigation and curiosity that is integral to a Caltech education.
The lectures were challenging to follow, lacking visual aids like slides or diagrams that are vital for grasping astrophysics concepts.
The pace of teaching was often rapid, making it tough to take good notes. Additionally, the classroom atmosphere became stressful due to the instructor calling on students.
The final was a 4-hour in-person written exam.
The workload for the observing project was significantly heavier than anticipated, and minimal guidance was provided. Despite these challenges, I would mention the non-mandatory trip to Palomar Observatory was really cool - probably the best part of the course outside the class.
