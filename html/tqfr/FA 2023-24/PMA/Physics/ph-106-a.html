Comments

What would you tell a student thinking about taking this course in the future?

It is a great course to take! The lectures are informative and the TAs are helpful : )
Maybe the best physics class I have taken here. The content is interesting, and Fuller's lectures are really good and informative. It also covers practically everything you will see on any sets or exams.

It is definitely a difficult class, but it is done fairly. There are more than enough resources to learn what is being taught well enough to apply on any work.
If you're taking this class, it's probably as a requirement, but honestly, this isn't the junior physics course to dread. It's hard, and if you're not committed to taking the class, it's not for you, but the content is very interesting, Fuller teaches in an engaging way that is worth going to lectures for, and overall, its one of the better physics coursed I've taken so far (as a current junior physics major).
It's a decent course that naturally requires a lot of self-learning.
The homeworks are long. Very long. Don't start them at the last minute because while they are not always conceptually difficult, they require a lot of algebra gymnastics. Also, online calculators like mathematica aren't allowed on the final, so be warned. You'll have to do a lot of ridiculous calculations by hand.
This was my least favorite course this term. The content was mostly fine, but the professor's policies and timed exams were horrible. The course mostly had problems with:
- Grade curves and grade communication
- Lack of willingness for clarifying comments
- Shortness of time on exams

For the first point, my understanding was that many people in the circle around me experienced a lot lower grades than what they're used to, both pre and post "curve." Seemingly even less than the previous years TQFR's. This was probably my lowest grade this term, and truthfully I think there should've been a stronger curve. For my own experience, I got a few points below the next grade cutoff, but found that my pre-curve grade was no different than my post-grade curve. This could be because I was high on the grade scale, but I don't think so. I unfortunately cannot compare because the instructor didn't release any statistics about the grades, which is a bit irking since he said there might be curves on both exams.

For the second point, I found that Professor Fuller was, in my perspective, a bit stubborn about clarifying questions. During a timed exam, I ended up taking a break to email a clarifying question (about a choice of coordinate systems) to the instructor. This went back and forth over whether this was a typo or something else. He said no it wasn't a typo and that he couldn't give me anything more. This went back and forth a few times until I gave up trying. Of course, he was correct that there wasn't a typo. I misunderstood a part of the problem and quickly was able to figure it out from there. My problem though is that 1. these are timed tests and 2. they're not testing us on how well we can understand a coordinate system/English of the professor's set up. The set up was very "quirky" to say the least. Which is fine, but on a timed test, it's not helpful, and it's even less helpful to refuse to even elaborate. It feels frustrating when what you're struggling on is not even the course content but the question itself and is, in my opinion, bad test writing/instructing. The instructors responses were at the least unhelpful, but truthfully felt spiteful and/or lazy (I'd like to clarify I first emailed a TA and then this conversation moved to the instructor, so that way he wouldn't have been bothered by a silly question). If this is wrong, then truthfully I don't understand why he wasn't willing to clarify.

Finally, timed tests. Simply put, there is far too little time on the exam. These exams encourage quick, mistake-filled, shabby responses over healthier, checked, quality answers. Worse on average than most Caltech courses. If you struggle with timed exams, I suggest you especially prepare for these ones.

Summary: Great content, great notes, great sets/problems, bad policies, bad professor, bad timed exams.
