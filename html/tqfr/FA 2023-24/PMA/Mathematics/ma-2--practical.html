The first half of the course was manageable, but things took a turn after midterms. Ma2 is definitely a class that you might find yourself struggling through far more than you thought you would. The class might start off easy with the first homework, but do not get stuck into thinking that it will just be easy. It gets difficult very quickly, as long as you are like me and have not taken differential equations before coming to Caltech.

As for getting homework done, like I already said, they increasingly more difficult as the term goes on, and often times you would need to go to office hours to find out what special substitution you would need to use in order to simplify/solve the equation. The recitations that were posted were very helpful for the first part of the course in understanding the concepts and getting the homeworks done. So either go to recitation or make sure you read the posted recitation notes.

As for exams, I found these to be extremely difficult. They are definitely meant to challenge you and unless you are keeping yourself up to date in the course, you might find it hard to even get started on some of the problems. Definitely prepare as much as you can for them, get everything you can out of the review sessions that the TAs host, and make sure you have a clear understanding of what is going on in the class.

As for lectures, you should really go to them. I made the mistake of thinking that I could get away without showing up to lecture. While for the first part of the class not skipping lectures was OK as I could still use the textbook to get through the homeworks, for the midterm, there was content on the test that was covered that week leading up to midterms. So although these lectures are in the morning and you think you can get away with not going, unless you have already taken differential equations before, then I would strongly recommend going to lecture. The main benefit is that then you at least have an idea of what content is being covered in the course and what you are expected to have learned, rather than just seeing what happens to be on each set and trying to learn just enough to answer the questions; this may not be so great when midterms roll around.

All in all, be prepared to dedicate more time to this class than what you initially predict, do not brush it aside, make sure to prioritize it as much as your other major core classes, and make sure to stay as involved as possible (going to lectures/recitations) to have the best experience possible.
