What would you tell a student thinking about taking this course in the future?

Super course! Taking this course has been a highly enjoyable experience, in every aspect. The problem sets have been not too difficult but still interesting. The lectures are excellent; Caniato is an amazing instructor who is really passionate about teaching. (I can personally attest to knowing people who have been motivated to show up to class just to hear Caniato speak.) The design of the course is also quite distinctive. We followed Rudin closely for the first half of the course, but Caniato took us on an excursion towards his specialty of functional analysis in the second half. While my understanding is that this topic is fairly atypical for a first course in analysis, I enjoyed it a lot, nevertheless.

Even if you don't like analysis, you'll probably appreciate analysis by the end of this course.

I have nothing but great things to say about this course! As my first upper div mathematics course, I was really pleased with everything about the course content, and especially Riccardo's teaching. The first half of the class covered topics out of Baby Rudin, and the last half of the course covered topics in two functional analysis textbooks at the undergrad (and maybe intro grad) level, so it was not your typical real analysis course. Riccardo is not only a wonderful lecturer, but runs his course very well and is a great person. If you have the option to take this class under him, you need to do so. As I am doing a math minor (CS major), I did not feel that I was out of place despite not having taken Ma 5. In fact, I think I would have enjoyed this course less if we had stayed on course with the content in Baby Rudin, since the functional analysis topics we covered were very insightful (and would have been nicer given a little more point-set topology background).
The assignments are pretty reasonable. Taking math 1c analytical helps a lot with building the kind of intuition you need to do these kinds of problems. The lectures are good; and, as with every class, you should attend them. The lecture notes are posted and they are a good resource, but they sometimes don't get posted for a few days.
I would say proof writing experience is required, but not 100% necessary if you have the requisite knowledge and are willing to work hard. But it's definitely a manageable course and it's reputation should not scare anyone from not taking the class.

Ma 108a was the most relaxed math class I've taken at Caltech, but it was also a fulfilling experience. We talked in depth about Banach spaces, Frechet and Gateaux derivatives, and so on; I'm really grateful to have learned about the Baire Category Theorem, as well as several other powerful theorems, through this class. Professor Caniato made the class self-contained and the topics approachable, so the textbooks were unnecessary for the class.
Very fun class - lectures were quite enjoyable and the textbooks were very helpful with the material.
Originally I thought this course was a bit too easy, but now I think it's just right and that more math classes at Caltech ought to be like this. There is quite a lot of redundant content from Ma1a (and extremely basic Ma5 content), so I hope what will happen is that some of that gets tossed and that there's more new content, while still maintaining the same difficulty.

What is however my favorite part about this course is that, unlike seemingly most Caltech math courses, problems on sets avoid "relying on some tiny trick." The problem sets make reference to source material, which I think is more accurate and helps shave off time for students working on the set. Most Caltech math problems, in my opinion, are made artificially harder by the amount of obfuscation they do of the relevant theorems/content. This course by comparison *is extremely refreshing* and I really hope Professor Caniato keeps teaching because of it. My other favorite part about this coursework was that, ***every timed proof (finals & midterms) was always provided a proof outline***. This seriously needs to be considered by more professors. It is absolutely bizarre to me that you can work on a single test problem for hours, and then have it become trivialized by one tiny observation ***on a timed test***. This is arguably my biggest gripe with Ma5, Ma2, Ma121a, Ma140, etc. Like seriously it's like being graded on an Escape Room and then finding out that you got a grade lower because you missed some secret code under some piece of furniture.

Simply put, Professor Caniato's Ma108 is probably one of the better math courses at Caltech now. You don't need to worry about obscure theorems and you don't need to worry about timing, as long as you put the hard work in. He helps develop your proof sensing skills without you having to do a trial by fire. I even wish I had just taken Ma108a over Ma1a.

The only genuine, objective flaw I can think of is that this course would really improve with some typed lectures notes rather than handwritten. They're a bit inconvenient, especially since you're constantly wanting to reference them. Other than that, great course. I'd even recommend it to non-Math majors, which is saying something.
