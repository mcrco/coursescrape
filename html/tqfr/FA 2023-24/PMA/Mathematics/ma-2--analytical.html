Comments

What would you tell a student thinking about taking this course in the future?

People told me that taking Math 2 Analytical was very unnecessary for a CS major. They're so right but also Math 2 ended up being one of my favorite classes and the only class that I didn't miss a single lecture for. I mean the class was still way overkill for my purposes but 10/10 would recommend.

Ok going back for a slightly more serious and helpful review.

The lectures are very well structured, and Hutchcroft's lecture notes (which are available online as of now) were organized clearly. As far as analytical courses go, I would put Math 2 Analytical on a similar level of difficulty and time spent as Math 1c Analytical. The class is primarily based around proofs as expected, but Hutchcroft does actively put some practical computational questions in to make sure we do know how to actually solve ODEs and not just prove that solutions exist or are unique.

The sets are difficult, but fair. They did consistently take more time than would I may expect from a 9 unit class, but all of the questions were directly relevant to material that was covered during lecture and built off of other proofs and examples in the notes. Grading was somewhat harsh, but still ultimately fair. Show up to lecture and read the notes, and you'll do fine in the class.

Hutchcroft was also responsive to student feedback and was helpful in answering questions. The only issue I had with the class was when we were given a midterm that we were told "should take around 3 hours" but took many students up to 9 hours purely because there was a lot of computational work to be shown. This was more of a one-time issue though, as I never felt that the work was tedious on any other set.

I highly recommend this class for those who feel comfortable with the workload in classes like Math 1c Analytical and those who are interested in more proof based classes for ODEs. It was difficult but enjoyable and manageable. Also now you can flex on other CS majors (or receive condolences).
If you fancy mathematical rigor and don't mind an expectation for precision, this course is for you. It takes a proof-based approach to differential equations, rather unlike most differential equations courses and textbooks that you will find. The content is fairly analysis-flavored, although the course is entirely self-contained. For me, the main source of difficulty I had in processing the material came from notational complexity. Stating theorems precisely requires at times somewhat complicated statements. If you can get past that though, the material is otherwise fairly approachable.

The lecture notes are clear and detailed and cover all of the things in the class. Nonetheless, Professor Hutchcroft's lectures add some value to the learning experience. He makes it easier to digest computations and grasp the important points, and he also explains intuition very well. Nonetheless, many people in the class are taking it with a course conflict and are getting by just fine without attending lecture.

At the end of the day, I wish more people would take this course in future years. It is certainly the case that more people are capable of handling this course than are willing to give it a try. I would stress that I found the class very well taught, the workload reasonable and balanced, and the material quite interesting; those eschewing the analytical section in favor of the practical section are missing out on an opportunity to learn differential equations in a unique, nonstandard manner.
Take the course seriously - it's the first class in the math core sequence that actually takes serious time and thinking to tackle. Ma2 analytical is also small and the students taking it are serious about the course, and Hutchcroft matches that, so it becomes a communal effort. The students in the course 2023 make jokes about being "Ma2 anal alumni" because of how difficult of a course it became.
This class is very interesting. The type of proofs that I did in this class were somewhat new to me, so there was some adjustment required at the beginning of the class. I spent a decent chunk of time on the homeworks, but nothing crazy until the last week, and much of the time I spent can be attributed to tedious write-ups as opposed to difficulty of solving problems (although there was usually one complicated problem on each homework assignment). Also, some of the problems require a fair amount of computation, so you should be prepared for that; but, if you took Ph 1b analytical with Prof. David Hsieh, the computation is not as bad as some of those Compton scattering problems.

The last homework assignment is probably the hardest assignment I have had at Caltech, although I don't think that it a fair comparison. It is definitely the most time I've spent on a problem set at Caltech, and I know a few other people who spent a similarly large stretch of time working on it. At one point ten out of the fifteen people in the class were working on the homework together. I don't know anyone that finished it. However, the reason why I say that this is not a fair comparison with other classes is that there is only one TA in math 2, and there are only 3 hours of office hours each week, so there is not a lot of time to get help. Luckily, professor Hutchcroft is understanding and he told the class to just submit what you have and he will adjust the grades. In terms of the overall difficulty compared with other courses I've taken, I think this class is a little harder than Math 5a and Math 108a. Except for the last week, it is pretty reasonable over all.

In terms of content, I think this was the most interesting class I took this term. As the name of the class suggests, we discussed a rigorous foundation of the theory of ODEs. In my mind, I breakdown the flow of ideas in the class as follows: in the first half, we built up to the main existence and uniqueness theorem for ODEs; and, in the second half, we found more techniques to study the behavior of ODEs and their solutions.

I have a bit of a love-hate relationship with this class. This course teaches you the theoretical foundations of ODEs and gives you a lot of cool, high-powered machinery to solve ODEs formally and rigorously. However, this is not without sacrifice. I spent 10+ hours on the midterm, 20+ hours on the final, and maybe ~6 hours on each set. And while the content is hard, if you did well in Ma1b/c analytical, then you should be fine. Also, I personally found the grading pretty harsh (and edging on petty at times), but it was also technically correct, so I can't really argue against it. Hutchcroft is a good lecturer, and as long as you focus your time/energy, you should do well in this class.
This was a pretty fun course but definitely took a lot more effort than the practical track. It was really cool to learn a lot of the theory behind ODEs which I probably would not have otherwise. The lecture notes were really thorough but had a few mistakes which could be confusing at times. As time goes on I feel like the lecture notes could be a whole textbook. Most of the sets were doable but pretty difficult (for a CS major). However, the last set was basically impossible and some people spent over 30 hours on it. This set should be fixed by next year though so I wouldn't worry about that. If you are willing to put in the effort I would definitely recommend to take this course, especially if you have experience with ODEs from high school.
