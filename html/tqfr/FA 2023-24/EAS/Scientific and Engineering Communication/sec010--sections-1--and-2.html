What would you tell a student thinking about taking this course in the future?

I am writing to provide detailed feedback on my experience in the SEC 10 course, with the intention of contributing constructively to the continuous improvement of the program. While I generally do not provide feedback on TQFRs, I believe it is essential to offer a thorough assessment for the benefit of future students enrolled in this section.

One overarching concern I would like to highlight is the pervasive disorganization within the course structure. In my academic journey, this has been the most disordered course encountered, and addressing this issue is crucial for enhancing the overall quality of future iterations. The grading system, comprising homework, attendance, and presentations, lacks transparency. Until the course's conclusion, only partial homework grades were made available, leaving students, particularly seniors striving for graduation, uninformed about their progress. A more timely and comprehensive feedback system is imperative to alleviate this frustration and promote a more constructive learning environment.

Furthermore, the feedback process for presentations has been notably disorganized. The initial attempt at individualized feedback involved cumbersome 1:1 meetings, leading to extended wait times. Subsequently, scheduling such meetings during finals week, when many students are departing, further compounds the issue. As a result, I remain unaware of my final grade, reflecting a lack of clarity and communication that detracts from the educational experience.

Another area of concern pertains to the course workload, which seems disproportionately heavy for a 3-unit class with only 2 hours of weekly instruction. The assignments, including extensive weekly homework and presentation preparations, demand significant time investment. Some assignments, such as a 90-minute YouTube presentation, exceeded reasonable expectations for a 3-unit course. Additionally, certain tasks, like researching the pros and cons of generative AI tools, lacked relevance to the course content, diminishing the overall educational value.

The content covered in class also raises apprehensions about its practicality for technical presentations. The repetitive nature of exercises, with minimal variations, provides limited opportunities for genuine learning and skill development. The insufficient time allocated for presentation preparation, coupled with the insertion of unrelated tasks, fosters an environment that does not contribute effectively to technical presentation skills development.

As SEC 10 is a core requirement, it is imperative that the aforementioned concerns be addressed to ensure a meaningful learning experience. Without these adjustments, the course risks becoming an obligatory box to check rather than a valuable opportunity for skill enhancement. I believe that with improvements in organization, feedback mechanisms, and content relevance, the SEC 10 course can better fulfill its role in fostering technical presentation skills.
