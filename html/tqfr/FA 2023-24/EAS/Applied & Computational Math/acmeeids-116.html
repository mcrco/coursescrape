What would you tell a student thinking about taking this course in the future?

Typical Zuev class, pretty similar in structure and difficulty to 104, which is to say very reasonable amount of work. 6 problem sets, 1 drop set, midterm and final. You don't need to go to the lectures because the lecture notes are very comprehensive so if you look at them in your own time you will be fine. I liked this class a lot
This class is a phenomenal introduction to how to think probabilistically, an intuition not necessarily intended to be built upon in Ma 3. The class starts off discussing random variables, expectation, conditioning, and the generalization of random variables as vectors. This framework is essential for the latter part of the class, which effectively pursues the theory of stochastic processes, namely Poisson processes and Brownian motion.

Homework: There are 6 sets with a drop set. The sets were very reasonable, with very little (if any) extraneous information needed to do the problems. The MATLAB coding portions are distinct from ACM 104 in that you write the entire scripts yourself, but it is exclusively simulation. Overall, not bad.

Exams: I felt the exams were somewhat tight on time, but as with Zuev exams, fairly reasonable given the content from lectures.

I recommend this class to anyone who liked the content in Ma 3, but did not understand why it matters to think of things in a probabilistic vs. deterministic fashion. A great class with enjoyable content all the way through.

This course focuses mainly on the applications of probability and statistics, with lots of examples from various fields, although some of the more simple proofs are covered in lectures. I highly recommend going to class because Prof. Zuev is a great lecturer. His explanations are very clear and intuitive, and he is receptive to questions. He does provide thorough, easy-to-follow lecture notes for when you have to miss class. I barely used the textbook because the notes were so good, but it was occasionally helpful for a homework question or to clarify something.

There are only six sets, including one drop set, and there are plenty of office hours to get help. Exams seemed to be appropriately challenging and fair. Most problem sets include a little Matlab coding, but there is usually a hint suggesting certain functions to use, and then you can look up the documentation for those functions.

I took this course at the same time as BE/Bi103A, and while one is not at all necessary for the other, I really enjoyed seeing the parallels. If you take that (or another course that uses Python) along with ACM116, just remember that exponentiation in Python is ** but in Matlab it's ^ (Python won't give you an error for ^ but you'll get the wrong result, as I learned the hard way). Overall, I highly recommend ACM116: it's well-taught and I learned a lot!
