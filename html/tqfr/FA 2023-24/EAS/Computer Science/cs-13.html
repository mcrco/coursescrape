Disclaimers: Frosh on P/F, course currently gives 70 drop points which will be changing in the coming years, had previous experience with proofs from FSRI Math.

I think this is an AMAZING class. Sure there were easier offerings that filled the same requirements this year, but this class really makes sure you understand how to do proofs and exactly what is expected of a good proof. The class also mixes in some extremely interesting topics from the field of CS and the proofs behind them. You get to see exactly why certain processes work, and Adam isn't afraid to argue with you on why something works even if it doesn't seem like it does. Adam cares a lot about each of their students and is very dedicated to making sure you understand the content as much as possible. They and their team of TAs aren't afraid to admit when they mess up either. They will make it up if they genuinely messed up on their end.

Use grading requests sparingly. If you genuinely think that they just mismarked a rubric item, request one. If you are asking for pity points, you are going to get denied, which is to be expected.

Attend every lecture. You really will benefit from EVERY lecture and learn some fun things along the way.

I went into this course after taking CS 21, and I chose it instead of Ma 6 because I wanted to learn more about the practical applications of discrete math to CS. I was pretty disappointed with this course. The material was overall very easy. The homework was divided into 4 "written" assignments and 3 "applications." The written assignments were all proofs. If you have experience with proof-based math, you'll probably find that none of the written assignments take much effort to figure out solutions to, and nearly all of the work is in writing up the solutions. The class has pretty high standards for the quality of the proofs, so if you're interested primarily in improving your proof-writing skill this class is a good option, but if you're comfortable with proofs you'll probably be disappointed by the lack of interesting problems. I also felt that the content of the applications assignments was a lot less interesting than it could have been. The applications assignments usually focused on a topic in CS (eg Huffman Codes, Naive Bayes classifiers) and involved a very small amount of coding followed by a series of proofs and/or written explanations. Most of the questions in the applications involved proving specific upper bounds, which got very tedious and didn't really do much to improve my understanding of the algorithms we were analyzing. Also, there was a lot less graph theory covered in the course than I expected.
Take Ma 6 or whatever the alternative to this class is if you're taking this class to fulfill the CS requirement. This class is not worth it; they grade hard so don't expect an A+/A unless you scored pretty highly in CS 21/are just good at math proofs. The only thing is that if you haven't taken CS 21, and don't know much about proofs, then maybe this class is worth it; I, personally, feel much more prepared for CS 21 after this class, but I had to work incredibly hard just to get a good grade because of the structure of this class. Plus, its experimental still; if you wanna do urself a favor and take something easier and have already taken CS 21, just take Ma 6.
