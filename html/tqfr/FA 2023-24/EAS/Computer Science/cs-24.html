Love it or hate it, 24 is 24 and an integral part of the CS option requirements. I don't believe that the class was as difficult as people had hyped it up to be, however, at times it did take a significant amount of time to complete some of the assignments (over 9 hours for a couple of projects), and was generally an interesting class in terms of projects and definitely nontrivial.

I think that the class was in some regards poorly organized/structured. One thing that made its debut this year was exercises meant to be done in lecture as a kind of pulse check. It was going well for the first few weeks and it was as promised where it was discussed in lecture well and submitting that basically got full credit. However, as time dragged on, some of them were rushed and poorly explained in lecture, and later on some of the concepts for them were touched on in class before moving on. Meanwhile, the grading rubric for them had become increasingly harsh and in order to receive full marks, one would have to include several 'keywords' or concepts which although related to what the question was asking or need to explain some parts of it in surprising depth given what the question was asking. As such, the grading rubric felt arbitrary and didn't reflect what students actually understood. Given the nature of these supposedly being "just a pulse check" and testing a little bit of conceptual understanding, it felt unnecessary how harsh these were graded in addition to it being part of a whopping 30% of the final grade in the class.

Additionally, throughout the term, on the course website, there were supposed to be readings and lecture slides to be posted to at least partially match what was done in lecture. However, past mid-October (note: about 3-4 weeks in), the only thing that was posted was one code example which was quite frankly absolutely necessary in order to complete the project (if one where to try to look up how to use signal.h / asm instructions to do so, they would never find this way otherwise) which was also posted relatively late in the week. It seems like it wouldn't be much effort to do so, especially given that most of the materials is the same as previous years, however, it was never done. Furthermore, the instructor had mentioned that they hadn't posted the slides intentionally, as they were upset that students that were going to lecture "weren't paying attention" and asking the same questions (perhaps they genuinely didn't get it or they were asking several clarifying questions on the same topic to get a better idea of what was expected to be written for the lecture exercises). Regardless of whether the students were paying attention, not posting slides for this reason seemed unprofessional to me, and although the readings were unintentionally not posted, it shouldn't have slipped past for that long.

As a final note, to this class's credit, it has a ridiculous number of office hours (something like >36-40 man-hours per week). However, usually office hours are crowded and it takes a while (35mins-1.5hrs) to get to the front of the queue and finally get help. In spite of all these hours, they're all clustered right before the due date, which wasn't helpful to me, as due to deadlines in other classes, I would more or less have to finish CS 24 early in the week, and hence, without office hours.

This class, depending on the week, may or may not take up a large part of your schedule. CS24 is a rigorous class (not as rigorous as CS 21), but this was probably the most involved programming class.

Unlike CS 2 and CS 3, where if one knows enough about the concept they can do well, CS 24 projects do require a somewhat thorough understanding of the conceptual material. Make sure to start projects early, as office hours can get quite full. In office hours, while I was in the queue for a longer time, I did spend most of my time debugging the problems I sought out to fix anyway.

Make sure you know what you are doing in terms of the project. TAs can give different advice for the same issue, so make sure you know what works for your implementation.

Make sure to attend lecture. This year, the course implemented "Lecturecises", which are in class questions given out by Adam at any point during the class. There is unlimited TA help on them during class, but once class is over, these become non-collaboration. However, there are few TAs for a very large classroom, so not everyone gets their question answered. While more time was spent in the beginning of year for lecturecises, there was less time spent on it, and the entire question was not fully given away in lecture, leaving students to figure it out for their own. The textbook was a good resource to understand the concepts though, so do utilize it when you need to. Lecturecises are worth 30% of your grade, so if you get a question wrong, you lose 1.3% of your grade (I think).

To answer a lecturecise, sometimes we have to look at notes from a prior year (Adam allowed this), because the notes were not put up on the website, starting from Week 3.

Concepts felt a little more rushed near the end, but the textbook was helpful for clearing such doubts.

Concepts are equally, if not more, important than the actually programming component, so make sure to stay caught up.

Overall, a very good class. I got much from it, albeit being one of my more difficult classes this quarter.
