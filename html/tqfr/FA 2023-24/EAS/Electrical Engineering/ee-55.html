What would you tell a student thinking about taking this course in the future?

This course is for major, it is tough. I think the best way to get the most out of the class is to look at the set when it comes out, gather ideas on the content from the week that is relevant to it, and make any attempt at starting the proof before office hours. Office Hours is also essential for this class, attend them religiously. Proofs are not my strong suit, but I found that, that method helped me understand the definitions on my own. I think the quizzes are pretty good for gaining beginner intuition. I do recommend reviewing the slides semi-thoroughly in order to score well. Even if you're unfamiliar with the topic I think taking the time to review the slides on your own really helps.
I would suggest any student thinking about taking this course to ensure that you are ready to commit your time to the course, especially if taking it concurrently with EE 44. FOR FALL ATHLETES: If you play a team sport during fall term, I would seriously consider not playing while you take EE 55 and EE 44 at the same time. This term is notoriously one of the most difficult terms for EE's, and playing a sport made it so much worse for me personally. I kind of fell behind in terms of knowledge early on in the term while my sport was in season, and I never fully recovered. Playing sports also makes it hard to work with your classmates and build a community for yourself. I felt so alone while my sport was in season because I would miss so many office hours and would miss the chance to work with people if there was a study session happening. I know that no one wants to take a break from their sport, but I think it is a valid decision to make under the pressure of this term.

On to the actual class: I highly suggest going to as many lectures and office hours as you possibly can. This class is proof-based, and building the intuition to actually do the proofs on the sets is extremely difficult. I think that even after completing the course, I still wouldn't be able to do a problem set on my own. Going to lectures and office hours will give you the context needed to complete the sets and a better understanding of how to solve the problems. The most frustrating thing about the class is that while I found the content interesting, it can be so hard to figure out how to do problems without someone explaining it to you. For me, most of the time it took to do the homework was me staring at the problem and reading the slides to try to find a definition or fact that would help me connect the dots and solve the proof. It was frustrating not being able to take my learning into my own hands, but talking to people in the class and going to office hours were extremely helpful.  

Like I said, the class is very time consuming. It is not worth it to push the assignments off until the night before they're do. I think that a good strategy to complete the sets is to start early. When you get a set, read through the questions. Even if you can't solve any of them, it will make you think about them. When you read through the set, note any definitions or other comments that come to mind. Then, go to the earliest session office hours and get clarification on what the questions are asking if you need it. Ask questions about how to approach the questions and if your ideas are correct. Then, go back to the set and see what you can get done on your own, and repeat. Looking at the set as much as possible is going to help you get familiar with the concepts, and it also makes for efficient office hours if you already know what types of questions you need to ask.

Finally, make sure to collaborate with your classmates. The EE population of each grade is not very high, and it's important to build a supportive community with each other.

Hope all of this helps! As hard as it is, you will get through it!

Ok introductory class. Professor Kostina is a skilled lecturer, and I found going to class essential. Her handwriting is good, her proofs are clear and easy to follow, and her slides are well formatted. The TAs were always prepared for office hours and graded fairly for the most part. Moreover, Professor Kostina and the TAs were very receptive to feedback, and often prompted us to share our thoughts on how to improve the class.

Content-wise, people seem to agree that the material covered was very abstract and hard to grasp. For example, one lecture, we covered typical sets. I could not name a single typical set until reading a Wikipedia article after class. It's not that the material wasn't covered —the slides showed a way to visualize typical sets as a "ring", and even gave the formula. It's that concepts are extremely generalized in lecture, and you don't see fully fleshed out examples until the quiz. If you're smart enough, your brain can probably fill in the gaps to go from the general case to specific cases, but I would have liked a little more guidance in this aspect.

To help with this issue, optional practice problems were given after every lecture. But these problems were also pretty abstract, asking us to declare facts about variables that we didn't have concrete examples of. Yes, we can consider a channel y = f(x), but what does that actually look like? It's left to the student to fill in the gaps.

To some extent this is not the fault of the professor but rather the material itself. This is a math class. Of course it's going to be abstract. Additionally, it's an introductory class, meaning there is a lot of ground to cover and we can't go into too much detail for any given topic.

While all the material is still a nebulous cloud in my brain, I'm sure that future classes will help solidify my knowledge, and I'll have an easier time because I've seen the concepts before in 55. I did learn some pretty cool EE applications in the process, like how we can compress signals by projecting them onto a Fourier basis, and how coupling BSCs can reduce error probability. Moreover, the promise of a massive curve kept me at ease throughout the quarter despite me having little idea of what I was doing. Overall, not a bad experience.
