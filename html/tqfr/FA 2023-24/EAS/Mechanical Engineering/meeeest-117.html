Comments

What would you tell a student thinking about taking this course in the future?

This class is probably one of the most fair and reasonably united upper-div electives for the ME major. The coursework is reflective of the lectures and the class is organized in an understandable and logical manner. The problem sets could be a bit "grindy" in terms of the amount of information you have to sift through. Questions were sometimes a bit vague so you had to pick a method of analysis and run with it at times. However, it is not a mathematically challenging course whatsoever and overall the sets were very doable. Professor Hunt also clearly cares a lot about the topic and that is reflected in the effort she puts into her lectures. Overall, anyone who is interested in Energy Tech and Policy should take this class. Even if one is not particularly interested, I believe it is one of the best classes to fulfill upper-div elective requirements.
For the most part, the assignments are fairly easy to complete and help us to engage with the topics discussed in class. There are four main homework sets with one additional non-collaborative homework set. These are supposed to be graded on completion, but expect a couple of points off for incorrect answers. Additionally, there are two presentations. One is a presentation on a current news article about energy and policy, which is graded pretty easily. However, the final presentation requires a lot more work and is graded on a much more difficult level. So, be prepared. Overall, this class is easy to understand and is a great introduction to energy and policy.
Great class, very interesting content, and a reasonable amount of work. Something I really liked about this class, especially in contrast to other advanced electives, was that it was very practical! We were studying many actual energy generation technologies/plants and looking at numbers and real data. The class covered a huge range of topics and scope, basically every current type of energy generation, with study of global energy budgets and a lot of specific information about California.

No exams, just homeworks that were not too difficult, and a 10 minute final presentation and brief write up about the same topic as the presentation.

Would highly recommend this class if you are looking for a MechE advanced elective that is interesting, actually useful, and properly united.
The class was quite enjoyable - the lectures had quite a bit of information I had not known and usually had information necessary to do the homework.
I found this class fairly interesting and quite easy. The sets are pretty straightforward (and there's only four of them), and any questions you might have are readily answered by TAs during OH. There is one non-collab set at the end that acts as a pseudo-final, though this set is generally based on problems that were in previous sets, making it pretty straightforward as well. The content is honestly rather superficial, and anyone with some domain knowledge in energy technology or policy is unlikely to learn a ton of new stuff in this class. The main curveball in this class is the final project (worth 38% of your grade), which is very open-ended, yet is graded quite harshly (as if those expectations were communicated to us beforehand). Be sure to pick a final project topic to which you can attach a sizeable amount of mathematical analysis, and also make sure to state all sources and rationale for assumptions made in your analysis (as any unjustified jumps in reasoning are penalized very harshly). Overall, this is a decent class that covers interesting (if not a bit superficial) topics in energy technology, and it is probably one of the easiest engineering classes at Caltech.
