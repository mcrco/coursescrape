Comments

What would you tell a student thinking about taking this course in the future?

Do not let the title of this class fool you. This is not an engineering class. If you were expecting to learn about the structure of satellites and spacecraft, then tough luck bud. Prepare yourself for a dull 10 weeks of geometry, with little to no physical applications until the last week or two of class. Now, let's go over the class a bit:

Lectures: Lectures for this class are useless. The professor's handwriting is illegible and he is literally just reading out of his textbook, which is really not a great textbook. Pages will repeat symbols or letters for different variables on the same page without differentiating them, typos riddle the book with errors, and the "proofs" are explained so poorly you'd think you're taking Ma1a all over again. These lectures read from the book are completely pointless to go to, as the professor provides nothing beyond what is already in the book.

The Sets: I hope you liked playing with glue, paper, and popsicle sticks as a toddler because you get to do it for this class! Each set has you construct a physical model, which is often extremely boring and time consuming, and seems to exist purely to make the problem sets EVEN longer, as if they weren't long enough. And what if I were to tell you that despite how unenjoyable these models were to make, the rest of the problem set was far, far worse. The content of the problem sets was often either only vaguely touched on in the book, with the set expecting you to put it all together from one obscure mention in the caption of a figure in the twenty third appendix of the book, to not being in the book at all. These sets took so long that it felt like working a full-time job.

Office Hours: With all of this, you'd think that the office hours would be helpful right? A shining beacon in the storm that is this class, saving us students from crashing into the shores? Alas, no my friend. Frequently, office hours would give unhelpful hints or no help at all, especially if you are an undergrad. Grad students seemed to get significantly more attention from the TA during office hours. Apparently, the grade disparity between the grad students and undergrads is so large that the professor wants to limit undergrads from even being able to take this Orwellian hellscape of a class. Honestly, good for them. Emailing the TA was also usually just as useless. Whatever hints you may get from emailing the TA sometimes directly contradicted the book, but due to the high volume of typos in the book, there was no way of knowing which to trust. On the last problem set, he refused to give advice on a particularly challenging problem because he feared "it may lead someone down the wrong path", just signifying that even the TA had no clue how to explain the problem. This was all, of course, assuming you even got an email. On one occasion, I emailed the TA several days before the deadline, and got a reply 5 minutes after the deadline. Helpful.

Grading: What about the grading? Surely with such a difficult class, it would be graded generously or curved? Haha… no. This class has some of the most draconian grading I have ever seen. The TA seems to fuel his superiority complex by being excessively harsh with his grading, taking off large chunks of points at a time. If you make a minor error but demonstrate an understanding of the problem, or the TA simply doesn't like your explanation, brace yourself to be docked 50% or even 75% of the problem's value. When confronted about this, he will either completely ignore you or send you an email scathing your work and saying that he was being generous for not taking off more. Sometimes, these messages came off as mocking your misunderstanding of the problems. However, I believe the worst thing the TA did was waiting until the day after Drop Day to post the grades for the problem set that everyone bombed. When we brought up these issues with the professor, he simply waved us off, saying that the TA handled the grading.

Put this all together: You have a professor who doesn't teach, a textbook that is useless, problem sets that don't reflect the class, and a teaching assistant that seems more concerned in punishing the students rather than, you know, assisting them. How can anyone in their right mind call this a class? How can anyone justify the existence of this kafka-esque nightmare experience?

Overall, I urge you: Don't take this class. please. If you value your mental health, sanity, and GPA at all, avoid it at all costs. There are lots of other electives that are interesting, will actually teach you relevant skills or theory that you can apply, and will be a much better experience than this disgrace. This is, without a doubt in my mind, the worst class at Caltech.
