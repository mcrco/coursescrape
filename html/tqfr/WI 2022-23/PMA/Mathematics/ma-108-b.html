Professor Nets is a wonderful lecturer. Assignment problems were interesting and tough however, professor Nets were very approachable and helpful doing office hours. There were 6 assignments with 5 good problems each. The course content very closely follows Wheeden and Zygmund's book on Measure and integral. It's a wonderful book to study this subject. Looking at the textbook before the lecture helps in a better understanding of concepts. Exams were easier than homeworks.
I would say having the textbook was very helpful as he followed it closely. Also you can do most of the homework in the 6 hours expected, but there will often be one very hard question, which will take you many more than the 6 hours to figure it out, if you even figure it out at all.
Katz is a great lecturer; he teaches at a good pace and takes care to explain hard concepts thoroughly. The assignments though........ are extremely difficult. Katz is helpful though and will give good hints during his OH.
This class focused on esoteric, minute detail concepts such as dyadic cubes to the detriment, at least personally, of the useful parts of this class--measure theory. Nets Katz' lectures are good but looking at the homework you realize you understand none of it. Praveen is a godsend.
It's hard to complain about this class, because I really did find the material interesting and relevant, and I ended up doing well, but I think there were a few problems that were just way outside of the scope of what should be asked in an undergrad class, and yet, I think some of them did yield really interesting knowledge when I worked through them by the end, so it is hard to say whether these were worth the trouble. Some of them I'm convinced Dr. Katz was just playing around with us though. Still, hard to really complain. I mean, it's Caltech, can I really be mad about a class being hard?

As far as logistics for any future students trying to learn about how the course is run, the text we used was Wheeden and Zygmund's Measure and Integral, and the course had 6 weekly sets, a midterm, and a final, where sets were usually (always?) 5 problems. The topics covered were mostly focused on measure in R^n, so a lot on Lebesgue measure, nonmeasurable subsets of R^n, and the Lebesgue integral of course. I think this class wasn't exactly what I expected since we didn't really talk more generally about sigma-algebras and stuff and focused exclusively on R^n but perhaps it was for the best as we got to see some interesting functional stuff (L^p spaces, Holder's inequality) toward the end.

Overall, definitely a hard class, but most of the difficulty felt pretty justified, and the final grading was not too harsh. Plus, if you enjoyed 108a, I think this class has a lot of the same type of thinking to it but is still very different.
Course ramps up in difficulty, but covers a variety of interesting topics. By the end of the course, you will definitely have a solid foundation in lebesgue measure theory and an introduction into Lp spaces. Be prepared to spend 10+ hours on later sets.
This class was surprisingly fun! Katz teaches analysis well and generally the grading was fair and reasonable.
