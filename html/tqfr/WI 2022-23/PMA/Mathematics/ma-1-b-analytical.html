Lectures are hard to follow/disorganized. As far as content goes, you will learn mostly the linear algebra basics (subspaces, independence, invertibility, row echelon) and emphasis on eigenvalues/spectral theorem. Little coverage on projections, dot products, and did not get to the cross product in this class. Take practical if you want to get a broader understanding.
If you are deciding between prac and anal for Math 1b, I would definitely recommend taking anal. Most of the course content is very similar, although anal has a little more proof-based rather than computation-based content. The homework is very manageable, especially if you go to office hours for help. (Neeraja is very very helpful!) Lectures can be fast-paced and a little disorganized, but they are overall pretty helpful. It is definitely useful to watch 3Blue1Brown's series on linear algebra to get a more intuitive understanding of topics.
I recommend taking Ma1b analytical if you generally like math and have taken linear algebra before (unless you REALLY like math). It's not too difficult but it is a bit more intense than practical. Time management is about the same as practical. Professor Graber explains concepts very well in my opinion.
If you have a decent math background, you will easily pass. You don't even have to go to lecture; each problem set gives the relevant chapters in the textbook, and you can simply read those to get all the information you need for the set. However, it will never hurt to attend lecture.

Solid proof based course. The problem sets were interesting but not impossible, and proofs were graded thoroughly and fairly. The quizzes were low-stress conceptual understanding checks (except Quiz 4, which had a huge number-crunching timesink).

Lectures were not essential (you could definitely do the sets without them, and most people did), but I never regretted going. When he wasn't proving theorems relevant to the HW, Professor Graber would go into different applications of the what we were learning: LaGrange interpolation, the Fourier series, Markov chains, plane conics…I had no idea linear algebra could be used for so many things beyond traditional vectors and matrices.

I would recommend Ma 1b analytical if you have some linear algebra background and want to take a deeper dive into the subject. It's a well organized, reasonable, and interesting course. If you're going in with less experience, the pass bar this year was a forgiving 50%, so you'll probably still be fine.
