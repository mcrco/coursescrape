Go to lecture and you will be fine. Don't go to lecture and you will probably struggle, since a lot of the necessary tricks to solve the problems are explained there. The course material is not exciting stuff; and E&M definitely can be really cool. The pacing at the end is pretty rough, since all the stuff you learn about electrostatics gets translated to magnetostatics in basically two weeks and you're expected to solve the same or higher level of problems in the magnetostatic cases. Otherwise it's really not as bad as everyone says, as long as you go to lecture - the sets, especially, are not very time-consuming and the midterm was reasonable. Final was hard because of the pacing issue mentioned - do lots of practice problems and it should also be fine.
This class was not as bad as I expected. Yes, it is a lot of work, yes the material is not trivial, but it is manageable. I have only one tip for you: GO TO LECTURE! It is very useful and Prof. Golwala specifically teaches you method of how to solve the homework problems. If you don't go to lecture, you will very likely get lost. Going to lecture and the eventual OH will swiftly get you through all problem sets. However, the midterm and the final are hard! Study up for them to learn where to look in his notes. Good luck!
Overall, the class is great for developing fundamental knowledge of electro and magnetostatics. The course content covers a good balance between Griffith and Jackson, and provides important mathematical techniques for solving Poisson's equation in electromagnetics. However, some students may find the pace of the class a little too fast.

This class is truly awful. Professor Golwala states at the beginning of the class that the material itself will never actually be useful in the sense that in the real world these calculations are never performed by hand. The point of the class is to "teach you how to think like a physicist." While by the end of the class I may have learned a handful of new techniques to solve problems, these could have been learned in a course where the content was actually useful and enlightening. Despite the professor's desire that we stay away from brute-force solutions with tedious algebra, this class involves quite a bit of it, as well as mathematical tricks that are difficult to come up with independently.

The structure of the class does nothing to alleviate these issues. Professor Golwala lectures very quickly, and missing even the first few minutes of lecture can make it impossible to keep up for the rest of the time. Even if you are able to keep up, many concepts are presented without sufficient explanation, and then the lecture moves on. The problem sessions are almost a complete waste of time. Several problems are presented, of almost the same difficulty as the homework problems, and we are expected to solve them in under an hour. The expectation is that the TA's will guide us through the problem solving process in order to prepare us for the set, but the reality is more often that the problems are posted on Canvas, people ignore the session and do other work until the hour is up, and then leave. Skipping these is not an option due to the extra credit offered for attending, so you just have to waste an hour each week going to them.

The only light in the darkness is that one way or another the class ends at the end of the quarter. And I do mean the end of the quarter, since Professor Golwala typically does not grant E's.
106b is a rough time. While most of the content is worth covering, and the material was cut down on even further compared to previous years', Professor Golwala presents it at a breakneck pace. For this reason I stopped attending lecture halfway through the term and found that it was easier to go through the lecture notes (which are quite comprehensive) at my own pace. There were also weekly "extra credit" problem sessions that I didn't find useful, but attended for the grade boost.

The sets are generally doable with collaboration, but there were some problems that were huge time sinks and required going to office hours for, so start early. Both exams were reasonable, but in light of the time constraint, my advice would be to go through the lecture notes beforehand and make a formula sheet that can be referred to during the exam.
Ph 106b and Ph 125b are brutal. The instructor is stingy regarding extensions and E grades and writes extremely quickly on the board. It is hard to follow in class unless I just don't take notes. However, there is an abundant amount of office hours for assistant on sets.
I think that for this course, it is really intended as a stepping stone for the undergrads who have taken the previous iterations of EM in the physics department, since it referred back to the topics of those classes heavily. As someone with very little knowledge in the field, it was a demanding class and moved very quickly. That being said, I am happy about the way things turned out, thanks to the great TA's and Golwala's teaching and resources. I have gained a great respect and introspection for the math involved in the class, and feel like I have learned like I never have before about how to scrutinize, analyze, approach, and manipulate mathematical expressions to make difficult problems into simpler ones through the means of extensive algebra and vector calculus.
All the material taught in Ph 106b is extremely important, and for that I am sorry the presentation of the class is as such: schoolish, inflexible and weird.
This class is not good at giving good intuition and understanding of electromagnetism. The lecture slides are very dense with material and often the important concepts and takeaways are buried beneath countless steps of algebra and mathematical tricks - one needs to spend a lot of time reading and re-reading the lecture slides to try and decipher and understand the key concepts. The HWs are doable but they are very long. While there is assistance available in the form of worked examples in the lecture slides and several office hours, the sheer amount of time spent outside class trying to understand the content and then complete the HW makes this class significantly more work than 9 hours per week. Another annoying aspect of this class is that the lecture notes often cover material from Jackson's E&M textbook and cite results from it, but this is a recommended (not required) textbook for the course, which means that we are not allowed to use it during exams - which is rather counter-productive. If we are covering concepts from Jackson's, then it only makes sense for us to be allowed Jackson on the exams.

The exams are also significantly more challenging than the HW (which are not easy to begin with). The extra credit from attending the problem sessions does a lot to help your grade, especially given the difficulty of the exams.

The class just feels like a chore and is very unfulfilling. You don't get a lot of intuition about the underlying physics, the mathematical tricks or the problem-solving approaches are often not well motivated and as a result, doing the HWs becomes an exercise in doing a lot of maths, and solving BVPs rather than learning and applying interesting physics concepts. The pace of this class is also too fast to foster a decent understanding of the course material.
