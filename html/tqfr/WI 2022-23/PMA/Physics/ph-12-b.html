Take ph 2b if Cheung is teaching it instead- I heard that was well taught. This class is not worth it if you are like me taking it purely for interest.
In this class, they teach you magic. No joke - the universe is inherently random, particles are waves, and energy is quantized. That's what they teach in this class, and to anyone without an advanced background in physics, that's just magic - period. This is why I thought the class was amazing! So if you're interested in having your mind expanded and warped, I'd recommend taking this course.

Regarding the course structure: the lectures were definitely helpful, but I'd highly recommend going to office hours every week. There are a lot of tricks you miss out on otherwise, plus it really helps build intuition when you can ask all the questions you want. The exams were definitely challenging, which is another reason why I found office hours to be so helpful.

It was rough but I got through it. I recommend looking up the youtube channel "Quantum Sense" and their series called Maths of Quantum Mechanics, it does a really good job of explaining the basics. Also, read Griffifths. It's a very good textbook, very readable imo. Try reading Chapter 3 before Chapter 2 if the notation still isn't making sense.
The textbook is essential both because the homework problems are mostly from the textbook and because it's useful for learning the material (clear explanations and lots of examples). I found Prof. McCuller's lectures difficult to follow, especially because he would write a lot of equations on the board and only explain the context verbally, and I had trouble connecting the dots. The exams were more difficult than the homework, but Prof. McCuller was gracious with adjusting the midterm scoring when most people felt it was too long for the time given.
The course structure was fairly standard, with eight homework sets (including one drop set), a midterm, and a final exam. While most of the homework problems were taken from the textbook, the textbook itself was excellent. It was well-organized, clear, and provided plenty of examples without being too dense. As long as you read the textbook, you should be able to do well on the homework assignments.

This year, lectures weren't super engaging, but they were still worth attending; Prof. McCuller taught content that could not be found in the textbook but was still covered on exams. He explained concepts clearly, and was especially good at answering our questions thoroughly, however, at times, he would get bogged down in just verbalizing math without providing enough physical context.

I highly recommend attending recitations since the TAs were fantastic at breaking down the concepts in a way that was easy to understand. They provided physical meaning behind the math concepts from lecture and taught useful tricks that made computations on the midterm and final exams much easier.
