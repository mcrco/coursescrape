Very well-structured class, noticeably high workload. Teaches good workflow for lab/research reports. Talking to both Professors was always very interesting.
If you are not a physics major do not take this course. This course teaches you a ton and accustoms you to long lab writeups. However, it is an enormous amount of work. Make sure to sign up for the right labs at the beginning of the course so that you are able to manage the workload effectively.
This is one of the hardest courses I've taken so far but also one of the most valuable. The workload is intense. There's a prelab, which is basically a set, due before each lab and a recitation the day/night before in which you have to go in and answer questions and explain the science behind the lab. Then, there's the three hour lab itself, which does take at least three hours, and the report every week after the lab. Then the cycle begins again. And, you're not doing the same lab as anyone else in the room. But, you learn a lot. I can't emphasize that enough. It touches on the physics of 1a-12b (and probably more, but that's what I've taken so far) and relates them. Honestly, there wasn't a single course I've taken at Caltech that didn't matter to this class. Scary but valuable. I've become much better at physics through this course.
The more time and preparation put into the lab the more you get out of it and the more you enjoy and appreciate the experiment you are doing. Most of the experiments are really interesting and insightful.
Good class, despite its reputation, I enjoyed it a lot and learned a lot about the physics topics being taught concurrently in Ph12b. My workflow was doing all the data entry and analysis in either Excel or Mathematica, then printing it out and taping it into my lab notebook, and handwriting my writeup around it. The most time consuming part was understanding the statistics and measurement theory needed to derive good results.
if you really like physics, this class is nowhere near as bad as the tqfrs make it out to be. I can understand hating this class if you're not a physics person, so don't take it if that is you, but the physics concepts and experiments in this class are super cool! frank and david are awesome and teach you a lot more in depth about what you're doing and are very helpful. they will grill you, but its all in good nature and they truly care about your learning, so dont be afraid to ask questions or look stupid. thats part of the learning process. the lab reports take a ridiculously long amount of time (I spent 10-20 hours on mine) but you can do more or less for them depending what kind of grade you're trying to get. it helps to reference the data analysis handbook. and make sure youre prepared for rec and lab. this class is super rewarding if you take time to understand and study the experiments. some good labs to do w relatively shorter analyses: exp 12, 19, 16, 25a, 28. avoid exp 14. lab will only be as intense as you make it, i found my lab sessions to be chill and enjoyable and an incredible opportunity to use cool equipment and do cool physics. i looked forward to them each week. its definitely a difficult class, but very worth it in my opinion! I would love it much more if the reports were shorter though bc those ate up a lot of my time.
I LOVED this class, it solidified my decision to be a physics major.
I have *absolutely* no bad things to say about this course. This course is amazing. Professor Rice and Politzer make one of the best combinations of professors you can ever have. One (Rice) makes the course light-hearted, understandable, and leads you to find the discovery yourself. The other (Politzer) explains the subtle ways of how the physics actually happens past what we know so far—which keeps the course extremely interesting, even when you *think* it's predictable.

I took this course solely because I wanted to (not a physics major), and honestly I think everyone should consider taking it as well as read the short accompanying text Data Analysis. This course is essentially the fundamentals of science and data analysis—something any researcher should know more rigorously.
If possible, start the lab reports early. Much of your time is spent organizing your data such that you are extracting the value you are looking for, in addition to properly propagating errors. While the TAs are evaluating your every week, some labs are tougher than others, both in theory and in practice. So don't be afraid to ask questions to clarify the lab procedure & report expectations before performing the experiment The TAs & instructors only want to help, as well as challenge, you. If you are open to the challenge & find the labs interesting, you will have a blast.
This was the best class i took this quarter, up there for best classes yet. The work is very reasonable, the labs are interesting, grading / TAs were chill. It is really clear that Rice and Politzer are very passionate and they always have a lot to say.
