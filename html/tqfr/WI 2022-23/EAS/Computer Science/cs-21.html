This was a really enjoyable class that I greatly recommend, even if you're not required to take it via the CS major! No *strict* prereqs, but I'd absolutely recommend a comfort with proof-based math as a problem-solving style, or a willingness to engage with that difficulty and learn from it. (*Very* minimal or no practical CS/coding experience needed, some familiarity with discrete math might be nice.) It's also just a more enjoyable class if you can balance your workload so that you're not starting sets the night they're do and pulling all-nighters. Taking this class as a frosh just to P/F it is definitely not the move, and does both yourself and the class a disservice.

Prof. Umans is an incredibly capable lecturer who makes even the most complex and mind-bending topics approachable and well-explained. He also clearly knows the field incredibly well, and every lecture was a delight. They're not mandatory attendance (although there is a participation score for 10% of your grade, in practice I think they give it to basically everyone), but I highly highly recommend attending nonetheless -- both for greater understanding of the material, and for the enjoyment of them.

There's one set per week, consisting of about 5 problems, as well as a midterm and a final (which are both no collaboration, but otherwise untimed and functionally identical to sets). We spend a few weeks on each topic of interest, which is more than enough time to get comfortable using them in a variety of contexts for the set. The sets are well-written, explore interesting topics, and do so in a meaningful and rigorous manner. Grading is decentralized across the TAs (there's also no head TA), so it can alternate between rather rigorous and nitpicky, to incredibly lax, within the space of a single set. In general, I'd err on the side of rigor in proofs!

The textbook is "Introduction to the Theory of Computation" 3rd edition by Michael Sipser, and very useful! The collaboration policy is also extremely lenient, allowing you to discuss anything with anyone, so long as you're not literally looking at each other's solutions. Office hours are generously scheduled -- they're basically all day Tuesday from like 2 pm to midnight or something absurd. (For context, the sets are due Wednesday at 1 pm.) TA styles and preparation for office hours vary, but it's generally all good.

Grading took the TAs about 2-3 weeks this term, which did introduce some uncertainty, but solution sets came out immediately, so it wasn't that bad. There's a curve -- relatively mild, but definitely a useful comfort!
