Do you have any constructive comments for other students considering taking this course? In particular, comments about workload/distribution of the workload of the course, the necessity of the textbook, unexpected time requirements or flexibility, or unspecified prerequisites could be particularly helpful.

This class is probably the most work I've put into a single course at Caltech. The class is divided into 5 2-week sprints that iteratively build on a basic spreadsheet model you build in the first sprint. The first sprint and first half of the second were probably the most work for our team because setting a good, clean base for your project is imperative for making things run smoothly down the line. Dealing with our project 1 fixes definitely took a good chunk of time out of the second sprint, but it helped us make sure we would score well on all subsequent projects.

Having a good team will also make or break the course. Toward the end of the sprints, we found ourselves working together for multiple hours almost every single night. So, make sure you're comfortable spending that much time with your teammates and can rely on them to get things done.

Overall, I really enjoyed putting the project together and the satisfaction that came with building an entire piece of software from scratch. It's a good class to learn the routines of producing software. Being comfortable with Python, Git, shell commands, and some basic software design (i.e. CS 3) is probably enough to get by in the course, but CS 38 is also a plus for some of the trickier graph algorithms.
Excellent course! Very practical course for developing real-world software. Pick a good group, ideally 3 students, and work together as much as possible so everyone is on the same page. Spend some time making good design decisions and plans before coding and think ahead -- this can save a lot of time
I think other students will provide good context for the ins and outs of this course in regard to content, so I will provide some insight to a group of students who may be unreached by other feedback: graduate students.

My group was three mechanical engineering graduate students, and we were able to be successful in this course even though none of us had any formal CS course experience. However, two of us had a few years of experience with Python and writing hacky research code (obligatory for grad students). If we had not had this prior experience with Python, we would not have been successful in this course. As such, if you are a graduate student in a non-cs field considering taking this class, you can be successful if you have previous experience with Python, otherwise it will be difficult for you. Also, as a non-CS person, I am very glad I took this course. I can already see how much my coding abilities have improved, and my research code will be vastly improved in the future. If you write code for your research that will ever be seen or used by others, this course is a fantastic choice.

Beyond all that, this was one of the best courses I have taken, and the projects and classes excited me in a way few courses ever have.

If you want to go into industry for CS after college, I highly recommend taking this class.

This class was honestly really great. Donnie's lectures about software engineering are good, and generally just helpful if you plan to go into the field; they don't relate to the projects much, but I kind of prefer that.

Work-wise, the focus of the course is on building a spreadsheet engine (this year in teams of 2-3) over 5 2-week projects/sprints. Make sure that you have a good group going into the course; I worked with friends that I know I work well with and, as a result, found the class really enjoyable! We spent an average of 9-12 hours per person on each project so, with lectures, I'd say the class was appropriately united for us. My only complaint is that assignments are graded pretty slowly; you get performance feedback quickly, so you can work on the next project no problem, but we don't have overall grades for any of the projects yet, and it's the last week of classes ):
Good class. Lots of code is written. Make sure you have a solid group going into it. Expect ~15 hours of work per person for every project.
If you think you will ever work in a professional software engineering environment, you simply *must* take this course! The lectures are an excellent overview of many hard and soft skills (e.g., git, repository management, code reviews, design patterns) that you will need in industry, and the assignments are essentially sequential layers of a term-long project (building a spreadsheet engine). The projects are a lot of work, but as long as you stay aligned with your team and start early, they should be manageable...our team of 3 probably spent ~8 hours per week per person on the projects, so the course is definitely a bit under-united...but all things considered, all the assignments felt worthwhile and rewarding (it was cool to see complex spreadsheet operations come to life from the code). Donnie is an excellent lecturer, and I would encourage you to come to class! Although the slides are posted online, Donnie often shares relevant (and amusing) anecdotes about the software industry that will be very relevant to you. Have fun!
