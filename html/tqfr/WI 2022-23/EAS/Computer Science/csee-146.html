Comments

Do you have any constructive comments for other students considering taking this course? In particular, comments about workload/distribution of the workload of the course, the necessity of the textbook, unexpected time requirements or flexibility, or unspecified prerequisites could be particularly helpful.

The class itself felt like I was doing three distinct classes - one on optimization, another on power systems, and then another where I was just doing a project.

Optimization portion: I took the class as an undergrad without prior optimization experience, since the catalog does not say you need it (although the syllabus does). Once the professor realized that none of the students had optimization background, he extended the convex optimization "review" to two weeks, and the TA also held additional recitations to go over the concepts. I ended up learning a lot about optimization from this - in particular, I felt like I learned a lot of useful tricks that I can use for optimization problems. But, the workload also felt very heavy for these two weeks since there was so much new content to learn. Later on in the course, we covered more optimization topics like semi-definite relaxations, robust optimization and chance-constrained optimization. These topics did not have corresponding homework sets, and were not really revisited later on in the course. As a result, I can't say that I retained all that much about these topics, aside from some level of exposure.

Power systems portion: First of all, I would not recommend taking this class without a solid understanding of circuit analysis. My biggest problem with the power systems portion was that a lot of the homework problems that we did for them did not feel like they were reinforcing intuition and concepts about power systems. Instead, they consisted primarily of algebraic manipulation and derivations of linear algebra facts that are arguably helpful for power systems applications. Professor Low would often mention in class that optimization could be applied to these power systems, but I don't feel like I got a very concrete idea for how optimization is actually applied to power systems, or even how power systems operate in the real world. For instance, I now know that Optimal Power Flow (OPF) problems are a class of problems that exist, but I would struggle a lot both in formulating an OPF for a specific application I'm interested in, as well as in actually applying optimization tools to solve that OPF.

Project portion: Professor Low makes himself very accessible outside of class, and also cares a lot about students. The project itself does not have to rely on the course content covered; in fact, I'm pretty sure that we all could have done our projects without using the concepts covered in the lectures and homework. My only real complaint about the project was that there were still two sets due during the second half of the class which was supposed to be devoted to the project. In particular, these sets took me 12+ hours each, which really limited the time I had to spend on the project. If your main goal for this class is to do a project with Professor Low and you don't need the elective credits, I would strongly recommend that you just sign up for research units with him instead.

The course as a whole is very flexible. Professor Low is extremely open to suggestions for the class, both since the class size is very small, and also since a lot of the course content is quite new. In fact, the course content sometimes felt a bit too new, since for one homework we spent hours trying to solve a problem only for the professor to realize that it was impossible. That being said, I doubt this will happen again.

While it probably sounds like I have a lot of complaints about the class, I think I got quite a lot out of it. For one, I got a decent introduction to mathematical optimization that wasn't as time-intensive as other courses. I also learned a lot about how complex networks can be represented mathematically, and the types of manipulations and mathematical tricks you can use for them. Both the instructor and TA were also very approachable and cared a lot about the class.

Finally, the course syllabus is posted online at http://courses.cms.caltech.edu/cs146/ if you want to give it a look over.
