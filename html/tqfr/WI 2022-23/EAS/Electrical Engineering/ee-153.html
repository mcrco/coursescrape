Comments

Do you have any constructive comments for other students considering taking this course? In particular, comments about workload/distribution of the workload of the course, the necessity of the textbook, unexpected time requirements or flexibility, or unspecified prerequisites could be particularly helpful.

Workload: The workload is very high, largely because Antsos forbids collaboration on any assignments. In exchange, he does not give exams but I would personally prefer the exams. The homework assignments end up being long and laborious. They often involve lengthy proofs that, if you get stuck because of some small algebraic mistake, you may not finish since you can't go to any of your classmates for help.

Course content: this course spends about a week on each of the following topics: transmission lines, scattering parameters, hybrid couplers, signal flow graphs, coupled line filters and couplers, noise, amplifiers and oscillators. Unfortunately, Antsos covers the material in a weird way that does not match with how microwave design is actually done in industry. I would highly recommend reading Pozar's Microwave Engineering text along with the class so that you learn how most people approach microwave circuits. In addition, the breadth of this class is much less than what Pozar's book covers. From what I hear, good microwave engineering classes at other schools will go through Pozar's entire book.

Teaching Quality: Antsos's lectures *can* be good. If you have learned some of the basics in another class, his review of transmission line theory, Maxwell's equations and S-Parameters is well--implemented. Towards the end of the class, however, it can become hard to follow. He never elicits participation from the students and so unless you are constantly asking questions, it is easy to fall behind.

Textbook: the textbook is a collection of notes from Antsos and his predecessor. It is incomplete as a reference all on its own. Again, I highly recommend Microwave Engineering by Pozar.

Summary:
- If you already took a microwave engineering class, don't take this one. 1) you probably won't learn anything new and 2) if you do learn anything you will probably learn it the wrong way.
- If you haven't ever taken a microwave engineering course and you won't be able to take it at another school in the future, this class is fine. Again, be sure to read Pozar so you get a perspective other then the one Antsos gives you.
- This class (labs and lectures) needs to be updated and while Antsos is teaching it, that will not happen.
I greatly enjoyed this class and learned a lot from it, but I had the luxury of taking this course as a graduate student, meaning I took the class P/F and my only other commitment at the time was research, no additional classes. If I were an undergraduate concerned with GPA, I would not take this course due to the amount of time I spent on some of the assignments, well above what was advertised. The workload was relatively light at the beginning, heavy in the middle, and medium at the end. I came in with no engineering background and I was ok, I would say the only definitive prerequisite is basic undergraduate EM.
Overall, I think this class had a lot of wasted potential that could have made it so much better. First off, I think the class would have been more enriching for students if the lectures were designed around Pozar or some other comprehensive microwave engineering textbook instead of Prof. Antsos' and Prof. Rutledge's textbook. Oftentimes, I would have to reference Pozar for content on the sets that was hardly mentioned (if at all) in the primary textbook in the class.

In addition, many of the design techniques encouraged throughout the class made little sense and, after just some short reading through Pozar, made even less sense. The methods explained (much more thoroughly) in Pozar were derived in a more intuitive fashion and were more robust than the design methods explained in the primary textbook.

The course policies were also quite strict, with an emphasis on completing all the work alone (with a strict no collaboration policy for all homeworks and labs) as a method of gauging student learning without the use of exams. I honestly felt like this detracted from my learning in the class since collaboration almost always brings new insights that are easy to miss when doing sets alone. TA responses on the homeworks were almost always no longer than a sentence per problem and did not help facilitate discussions for better coursework understanding.

The one saving grace in this class was the laboratory sections, where I feel like I learned the most. Antsos's teaching style in lectures, while incredibly thorough and intuitive, sometimes glossed over some content necessary for lab understanding/completion. However, Kiran would take his time to thoroughly explain the lab every Wednesday and would often dedicate his entire day to make sure everyone understood the lab and finished their boards. His lab sections were honestly the highlight of my week during a very rough term, and he was just overall amazing.

Overall, besides the weird collaboration and grading policy (which forced me to take this class on pass/fail in the end) and the subpar primary textbook, this class was amazing and honestly has the potential to be even more worthwhile with some small changes.
