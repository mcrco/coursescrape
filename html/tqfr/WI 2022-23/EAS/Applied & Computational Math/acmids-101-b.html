Do you have any constructive comments for other students considering taking this course? In particular, comments about workload/distribution of the workload of the course, the necessity of the textbook, unexpected time requirements or flexibility, or unspecified prerequisites could be particularly helpful.

The workload can seem high, but considering you only have to turn in one question on each HW, things are much more manageable. You don't need a super high background knowledge going in and the recommended textbooks help a lot to fill in gaps.
-
While for graduate students trying to balance classes and research it may be smart to target specific, approachable problems on the sets, I do recommend always taking a full stab at all of the problems on the set. There's a lot of interesting material, and even just noodling over a problem may help you find connections to other important problems in your research.
Bruno continues to be an excellent instructor - lectures are 2 hours long but stay engaging with Bruno's enthusiasm for the subject.
Can take this independently to ACM101a - although they are some cool links.
In my opinion, ACM 101a was much more interesting, had more new content, and was also much easier to understand than ACM 101b. ACM 101b often felt like a repeat of ACM 95b, but with a focus on all of the gory details that we didn't have to get into in ACM 95b whereas asymptotic expansions in ACM 101a were genuinely a new concept to me (not covered in Ma 1, Ma 2, ACM 95, or various physics classes). Professor Bruno is an excellent instructor, as always, it's just that the content didn't do him justice. On the bright side, ACM 101b was at least not a stressful class, since we only needed to turn in one question per set (i.e. the highest score on a question was the score for the entire set), there were no exams, and there was even a drop set.
Professor Bruno is still great, but the content in this class is much less interesting than ACM 101a. There's a lot more coding/computational work too than in 101a.
Be ready to do interesting and difficult work. A very rewarding experience overall!
An extension of 95b this time. The content was more difficult than 101a and still very interesting! 101b has a some more implementation problems than in 101a. There's a surprising amount of overlap with ACM 106b which was nice and helpful. But it was more so going in depth on why the methods in 106b worked. Towards the end, we covered a few electrostatics PDE problems which was fascinating. Same homework policy as before too. Both TAs are helpful for the HWs.
ACM101b seemed more straightforward and much less overwhelming to me than ACM101a because it covered topics more similar to other ACM core courses, including Fourier series/expansions from ACM95b, some linear algebra from ACM104, and some numerical approximation methods from ACM106b. As in ACM101a, Oscar is a phenomenal lecturer in ACM101b and the deep dive into PDE theory can be very fulfilling for anyone interested in PDE/ODE adjacent research fields. Overall, the workload is reasonable and the content was enjoyable and very much increased my interest in PDEs.
It is a fine course with a great lecturer. If you've taken ACM 106A this course should be pretty straight forward.
This class was very interesting and gave an cool, different perspective on numerical PDE compared to ACM 106b. We had a policy where the grade for each problem set was the same as the best problem done on the set, which made the workload much more bearable.
