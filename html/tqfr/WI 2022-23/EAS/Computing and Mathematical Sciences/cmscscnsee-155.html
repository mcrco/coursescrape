I would not recommend this course. The lectures are not clear in terms of conceptual organization and flow (especially compared to CS156A), and the mathematical details are presented before building intuition. If you do take the course, I would recommend not bothering to watch or attend lecture, but skim through the slides and fill in the gaps elsewhere. To the current JPL instructors' credit, they did not design the course content. The course staff is very responsive and helpful in general.

The latter homework assignments are excessively long in my view and also coincide with two projects, so the course is notably back-loaded. The homework questions are broad but the expected answers are very specific, so it often feels random whether your explanation happens to have what the rubric is looking for.

If you are looking for a machine learning course and have not taken CS156A, I think it is a much better course, builds more intuition, and covers most of the necessary machine learning concepts. The rest I would recommend picking up through another means.

While I had issues with the course organization itself, the material in this course was very interesting and the projects were pretty fun. The projects were more manageable, but I was very fortunate to have a group where we all pitched in. However, I could not imagine doing the entire project on my own. The lectures were fine and helpful, though personally, I preferred learning through other online resources. If this course is taught and organized similarly in the future, I recommend making sure that your schedule allows for a lot of time to be spent on the sets and projects for this class.

I was extremely disappointed with this class.

My understanding is that the purpose of this class is to survey many of the major topics and concepts in machine learning. I personally watched the lecture recordings from a previous year (to find them, search "caltech cs155 lectures" on YouTube). I thought the lectures did a solid job of giving me a high-level introduction to a wide array of topics in ML.

Unfortunately, that was pretty much where the learning stopped for me. I thought the homeworks took an unreasonably long time, and they really didn't help me understand the material much better. I spent a substantial amount of time on the assignments for this course every week, and I feel like I got very little back for that time. So, my personal summary of the class would be this: it takes up a lot of time, and leads to little learning.

The main exception to this was project 1 (which was a Kaggle competition). I thought project 1 was a lot of fun, led to quite a bit of learning, and helped me get a feel for how to apply what we learned to a real-world problem.

Despite this, if you're interested in ML, I would recommend this course. Not because I think it's a good course, but just because if you're interested in ML, you don't really have much of a choice. This is the only class that covers a lot of essential content in the field.

In terms of prerequisites, I don't think there's anything too serious you need (other than Python programming basics and linear algebra basics). It would certainly help to have taken CS156a and/or to have some ML experience, but I don't think it's necessary at all.
