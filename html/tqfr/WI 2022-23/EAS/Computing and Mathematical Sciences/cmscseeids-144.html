Do you have any constructive comments for other students considering taking this course? In particular, comments about workload/distribution of the workload of the course, the necessity of the textbook, unexpected time requirements or flexibility, or unspecified prerequisites could be particularly helpful.

This course was very difficult, but very rewarding. I think I learned a lot and looked forward to lectures.
This course is a lot of work because it has a lot of different components: sets, blog posts, two mini-projects with leaderboards, and a project proposal. The sets are split between proof-based (varying difficulty) and implementation-based (usually pretty straightforward). This class is _actually_ 12 units.

The first project (Pandemaniac) is a lot of fun if you can clear out a week or so to put a lot of work in. Otherwise, it seems to drag on, you feel rushed, and won't get much out of it. The second project (Pandemaniac++) is new this year. It was fun, if a little unclear, but was much less work, which was welcome. For me, the easiest way to complete the blog posts was to report on something I already knew about, so I didn't learn much from completing them.

I was very excited for this course going into it, but it seemed to have a difficult time tying its concepts together. A lot of time is spent talking about properties that networks exhibit in the real world. Problem sets examine random models of real-world networks and ask you to make a lot of bounding arguments. The latter half focuses on game theory, auctions, etc. I think both halves are interesting, but they weren't tied together very well. It's also not clear how any of this actually becomes useful beyond "huh neat a lot of networks kind of look the same" and "games with imperfect information or information markets are really hard." While we came close to this with blog posts, it never seemed like we got past a surface-level engagement: it's not clear what concepts from the class actually helped you understand any of these practical applications. Similarly, the projects had you ponder how to leverage these learned ideas, but what it really seemed to teach is how far theory is from practice on these sorts of problems. Trying to not spoil anything, it seemed that the best solutions weren't very interesting through the lens of what we learned in class.

Looking back at what I'm leaving this class with, I can say I'm much better at making mathematical bounding/big-O-type arguments when randomness is involved. I know a few more maxims about networks, and the names of a few ways to characterize them. I know more about game theory and auctions. Maybe this is the nature of a class making a brief foray into a large field, but I don't feel like I can now do anything I couldn't before. For a point of comparison, I felt more that I actually gained new skills or learned something after CS21, CS38, CS124, CS142, and CS171. The payoff-to-work ratio wasn't there for me personally.

All that said, the lecture content is very interesting, so I can overall soft-recommend. Adam is a great lecturer, and I hope this class keeps the same energy in the first half when the professor transition is complete. Hopefully they tweak the workload and projects for next time.
