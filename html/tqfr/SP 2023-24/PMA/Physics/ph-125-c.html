Comments

What would you tell a student thinking about taking this course in the future?

Professor Cheung is one of the best physics lecturers I've had in all my time as an undergraduate and now graduate student. His lectures are engaging and interesting, more like storytelling than anything else. He goes over the basics just the right amount before delving into the more complicated topics in a way that helps strengthen your intuition of quantum.

I still needed a good amount of help from TAs on the homeworks, and the midterm and final were doable, but not at all easy. But it was the kind of challenge that you appreciate because he does give you the tools to succeed. The key to the course is paying close attention in lecture, because he truly covers everything you need on the assignments in there, and organizes the material better than the textbooks do.
For the chemistry students interested in taking Ph 125c: I encourage you to take this course, but you should be cautious because unlike Ph 125ab, there are timed exams in 125c, and the structure of the course does not follow any particular textbook. The topics in Ph 125c are useful for anyone interested in solids and (to a somewhat lesser extent) in spectroscopy.

Here are the textbooks you should consider pirating. You won't ever need to read through the whole thing for Ph 125c, but you might need to CTRL+F for minutiae in them, or might want to read them thoroughly out of interest:
- Quantum Mechanics and Path Integrals (Feynman)
- Supersymmetry in Quantum Mechanics (Cooper), or this article: https://arxiv.org/pdf/hep-th/9405029
- Introduction to Superconductivity (Tinkham)
- Advanced Quantum Mechanics (Sakurai) [note: this is different from Modern Quantum Mechanics, also by Sakurai!]
- Any QFT book
- Any condensed matter book
- David Tong's notes

Overall Ph 125c, I think, has two goals:
- Introduce students to interesting phenomena that are related to current research (Aharanov-Bohm, superconductivity, etc.).
- Prepare students for future courses that apply quantum mechanics (Ph 135/223, Ph 205, Ph 219).

Unlike in Ph 125ab, the problem sets in Ph 125c are significantly shorter, but consequently also do a worse job at preparing you for the final. You should do extra practice problems if you want to do okay on the final - it is probably curved, but I'm not sure to what extent. I think this course is sometimes offered on only pass-fail, but this year it was offered on grades (with optional pass-fail).

Something weird you may notice if you read Griffiths, is that a lot of the topics talked about in Ph 125c are basically harder versions of the challenge problems in Griffiths. So if you're a student who has done (or is doing) Ch 21 or Ph 12 and you intend on doing Ph 125 in the future, you might want to consider doing these problems to get an exposure to the ideas in Ph 125c: 3.42, 3.47, 4.43, 4.44, 4.59, 4.75, 5.38, 7.57, 12.3, 12.4, 12.7, 12.8. Numbering follows the 3rd edition.

The lectures are very good. The problem sets are much shorter than the ones Frank Porter assigned. The course covers an array of topics, including: path integrals; the imaginary-time formalism; Bell's inequalities; the quantum Hall effect; the Aharanov-Bohm effect; and, the dirac equation. (This is not a complete list.)

The recitation sections go over advanced topics, which are pretty interesting.
Problem sets tend to be very easy, but the midterm is time-consuming and the final is especially difficult. I came out unscathed (I believe 85-90% is an A-), but take on grades at your peril.
