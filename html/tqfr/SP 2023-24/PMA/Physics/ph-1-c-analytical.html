Content of the course is great, very interesting to learn about. I'm not a physics major and took this class (along with Ph 1b Analytical) because I was just interested in the content. Lectures are great, it's pretty important that you go to as many lectures as possible if you aren't familiar with the content or can't learn well from the textbook (the lecture style is also great). Recitations are also super helpful. But bottom line, the class is hard and you should go into the class expecting it to be harder than Ph 1b Analytical. It's definitely doable, but don't expect it to be light (if you're someone with basic prior experience with physics, like me). Some of the homeworks and quizzes can be really tough, but this class prepares you really well for more difficult things to come at Caltech. Would definitely recommend.
More so with physics than math, I think taking analytical is worth it. If you want to make life easier, sure take practical. But, as I see it we came to Caltech to learn as much as we could! I am not so familiar with what Practical does, but I do know that you learn a lot less of the theory behind electromagnetism than in Analytical. It really is a shame given how beautiful the theory is. I think that going through the course will be helpful for any student here; you use a lot of vector calculus and become quite proficient by the end of term. That's always a practically useful skill to have even independent of the physics theory you learn in the class. So that's my take - take analytical!

Textbook: Electricity and Magnetism, Berkeley Physics Course, by Edward Purcell and David Morin (3rd edition) (Chapters 5-11).

Lecture 1: Magnetism -- Introduction; Relativistic origin (Ch. 5.1 - 5.9)
Lecture 2: Magnetic field (Ch. 6.1 - 6.2)
Lecture 3: Vector potential (Ch. 6.3 - 6.4)
Lecture 4: Magnetostatics (Ch. 6.5 - 6.6)
Lecture 5: Lorentz transformation of Fields (Ch. 6.7 - 6.9)
Lecture 6: Faraday's Law (Ch. 7.1 - 7.3)
Lecture 7: Induction (Ch. 7.4 - 7.5)
Lecture 8: Inductance (Ch. 7.6 - 7.10)
Lecture 9: AC circuits (Ch. 8.1 - 8.2)
Lecture 10: Impedance (Ch. 8.3 - 8.6)
Lecture 11: Displacement current (Ch. 9.1 - 9.2)
Lecture 12: Maxwell's equations (Ch. 9.3)
Lecture 13: EM waves (Ch. 9.4 - 9.7)
Lecture 14: Electric dipoles (Ch. 10.1 - 10.6)
Lecture 15: Dielectrics (Ch. 10.7 - 10.9)
Lecture 16: Electric fields in matter (Ch. 10.10 - 10.15)
Lecture 17: Magnetic dipoles (Ch. 11.1 - 11.4)
Lecture 18: Magnetic materials (Ch. 11.5 - 11.8)
Lecture 19: Ferromagnetism (Ch. 11.9 - 11.16) (optional*)
Lecture 20: Applications; Loose ends (optional*)
* All lectures were optional, but the last two lectures didn't cover content in the problem sets / exams.

Lectures were each Wednesday and Friday, and homework was due 11:59pm each Wednesday night. Dr. Patterson is incredibly clear in his explanations. There were 4 quizzes (1 every ~2 weeks). Each quiz was 2 or 2.5 hours and open notes. There was no midterm, but there was an open note final.

I didn't go to many TA office hours or recitations (I really should've). I didn't like a few of the TAs, and it was difficult to fit the rest into my schedule.
I had a course conflict and decided to attend the lectures for my other class rather than this class. The only instruction that I received in this course was from recitation (which I attended about half the time).

Initially, I was a little worried about how well I would understand the material without going to lecture. It turns out that everything worked out fine. Each week, I acquainted myself with the previous week's material through the problem set. Afterwards, I would go back and read up on the material in the corresponding chapters of Volume II of Feynman's lectures in physics, which deepened my understanding of the content and helped me do well on the assignments. As I followed along the course with Feynman, I developed a great appreciation for his writings and explanations.
