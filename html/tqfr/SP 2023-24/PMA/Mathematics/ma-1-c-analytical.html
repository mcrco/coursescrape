This class is organized in a way that roughly follows Spivak's Calculus on Manifolds. The precise organization of the class follows Tony Yu's own lecture notes very closely. The lectures directly follow the notes, and Tony usually provides helpful figures / examples that are sometimes not reported. The notes losslessly cover all the necessary content for the course. Recitation notes are posted and also directly correspond to the content covered in recitation, which usually provides worked examples helpful for the sets / exams.
The first two-ish questions on each set usually are very computational - things that feel like they could be covered in prac. Solving these questions usually feels like applying definitions from the notes and churning out algebra. There are also a few questions that usually involve the "higher power" ideas of the class - these proof-based questions are really instructive and apply the ideas of concepts. Often, these questions have ideas that are never directly addressed in lecture, but you can usually find analogues or worked examples that help in one of Spivak (Calculus on Manifolds), Rudin (Principles of Mathematical Analysis), or Zorich (Mathematical Analysis). These external works, especially Spivak and Rudin, often give a little bit more content that supplements the bare definitions from the lecture notes.

The first half of the course develops an idea of Euclidean spaces, differentiation / continuity using topology, and the inverse and implicit function theorem. The midterm was roughly aligned with the difficulty of the sets (indeed, it was just set #5, just with a time limit). After the midterm, we covered multivariable integration with the Riemann integral, and then developed differential forms and techniques of integration (and at the very very end, classical vector calculus). Differential forms are not introduced in a very intuitive way - to me at first, they felt like applying definitions and theorems to solve problems on the set. However, the longer they stew, the more intuitive they start to become. It really helped also to skim through "A Visual Introduction to Differential Forms and Calculus on Manifolds" by JP Fortney, which presents - in detail - many equivalent definitions and worked examples of differential forms and their theorems. Many computations necessary for the sets - which aren't fully worked out in lecture - are explicitly worked out in Fortney. He also presents the important results and gives several equivalent formulations of them, which is very helpful for building intuition. The final involved mostly computation, applications of theorems already worked out. (Also, there were a free 5 points on both the midterm and final for filling out the midterm course feedback form / final TQFR :).

The very last theorem explored in the class was Stokes' theorem. From Spivak: "The proof of this theorem is an utter triviality - a straightforword computation. However, even the statement of this triviality cannot be understood without a horde of difficult definitions." It feels like all of the material of the class simply builds up up to the generalized Stokes' theorem - then, the last week is just applications. All of classical vector calculus is given one lecture at the very end. It didn't really feel like I really understood any real multivariable calculus until this last week :)

This class is primarily a differential geometry class, taught through the lens of topology. The sets were 40% trivial computations (that we never learned how to do, like line integrals), 40% tedious computations (that would take a page or two of busy work), and 20% topology / differential geometry proofs (which were usually very obvious and thus a waste of time to prove). However, it never felt like we were well equipped to answer any of the questions on the sets; as I mentioned, we were never taught how to do any of the computations that you would expect to learn in a multivariable calculus class, like line/surface integrals, vector calculus identities, multivariable Taylor expansion, etc.
