Comments

What would you tell a student thinking about taking this course in the future?

This course is a bit of a let down from Ma5a and Ma5b. Ma5a, taught by Dr. Gherman, was beautiful taught and he truly believed in student success. Ma5b was taught by Aluffi, the material was interesting (although a bit terse to people without sufficient math background), and just challenging to be enjoyable. What makes this class a let down from these parts of the sequence is that it's not as well planned/thought out, and student success is very clearly not prioritized in the catering of the course. Moreover, way too many times, the teacher would forget to change the Homework title to "Ma 5c" instead of keeping it to be "Ma 120b" (ding ding ding! you guessed right, he would just remove 1-2 problems from the 120 b homework and call it out 5c assignment). Well this was definitely an interesting strategy... if that's not funny enough the 120b final was uploaded in place of the 5c final for an entire 1.5 days.
Since I've really enjoyed my other classes at Caltech, I have to say this class was probably the "math core" equivalent of Ch 1a. It was really let down by how disorganized and boring it was. It made me hate field theory than I already did.
This was a pretty solid class üëç. We followed the Ma 120b notes for the first half of the term to learn Galois theory, and worked through JP Serre's "Linear Representations of Finite Groups" for the second half of the term to learn representation theory. I found it slightly easier than Ma 5b, and slightly harder than Ma 5c. I recommend going to office hours as well!
The homeworks are not too hard, but they get pretty tedious in terms of computing splitting fields and Galois groups.
The concepts are difficult but the grading on homework was extremely lax when I took it. The last third of the class was about group representation theory for like no reason and IMO this was pretty lame.
Ma5c is the pinnacle of the Ma5 sequence, and I highly recommend taking this course for anyone who finds Ma5a and Ma5b enjoyable. Proficiency with group theory is a must, and I would strongly advise acquiring experience in abstract algebra from 5a and 5b prior to taking 5c. The content, namely Galois theory and representation theory, is at a somewhat higher level of abstraction than group theory or ring theory; however, overcoming that challenge is highly rewarding, for it yields many surprises with interesting discoveries to be made along the way, and it promises to unveil mathematics with a degree of structure and elegance that is seldom surpassed.
