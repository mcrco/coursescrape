What would you tell a student thinking about taking this course in the future?

As a mechanical engineering student, you'll have to take this course. However, I feel as if you should get acclimated to the content before attending the class as the pace of it can be quick at times and there is just quite a lot of information that you get through in just the first few weeks. The Navier-Stokes equation is bomb dropped on to you in the second lecture, which can be daunting. For me personally, I never quite grasped the meaning of these equations until I had time to study them (which there is never enough time here at Caltech). The book is helpful only a handful of times, but other than that is very boring and monotonous to read and ceases to be useful in most of the sets. You should rather learn how to learn: being able to do active recall and defining certain values (Reynolds number or shear stresses or partial derivatives) and mentally connecting the concepts you're learning with your real-world intuition on how liquid moves as well as connecting the equations that you learn and breaking them up into every term. Easier said than done of course. Make sure that you retain some material from ME11b, as there are a few thermodynamic concepts that will be relevant in the latter part of the course with heat transfer and isentropic flows. Biggest thing is to learn to time manage and start your homeworks early of course.
It's significantly more math-intensive than ME 11a or b, but I also found the material more interesting and the problem sets more engaging

To start off, ME11c has been one of if not my favorite class at Caltech so far. Of course, I might be biased as I am interested in fluid mechanics, but this class really felt like a great way to conclude the ME11 series and I feel as though I really took a lot from this class.

The class starts off with a derivation and exploration of the Navier-Stokes equations that govern fluid mechanics, and then branches off for the next couple of weeks into solving them for a couple of common internal flow setups. We then transitioned into external flows and looked into understanding the boundary layer, and then learning about potential flow, both of which are extremely cool. The math in these topics can get a little abstract through the use of non-dimensional analysis and some of the vector calculus. Luckily, the lecture notes are very thorough and, again, the homeworks are designed to help guide you. After the midterm, the class seemed to shift gears a bit. We then started with a unit on heat transfer, which was really an extension of what we learned in ME11b with more of the math behind it now getting involved. Then, we transitioned into compressible flow. Here, if you are at all interested in fluid mechanics/Aerospace Engineering, is where you will really learn a lot of applicable things. To start, compressible flow just introduces a whole new behavior of fluids than what you found in the incompressible cases. Secondly, in this class we went through a bit of rocket nozzle theory and understanding shock waves, which, as you can imagine, were very cool and applicable. We then closed with a lecture on geophysical flows.

The first couple of homeworks were math-heavy, but after that we started to encounter much more practical aspects of fluid mechanics. The homeworks in general can take a bit of time, especially since they almost always had at least one mathematical derivation question, so I really wouldn't recommend leaving them to the last minute. What was also nice was that the subparts of questions were really there to guide you, and the posted lecture notes were great for referencing to get answer the homework questions (as well as just for general studying of the material). Lastly, like the rest of this course, the homeworks balance theory/math and practice well. Especially towards the last couple of homework sets, while there was some math, the problems were also very practical in nature.

The exams for this year were a bit tough, so I would recommend that in addition to using the lecture notes for getting the homeworks done, that you also spend time throughout the quarter trying to internalize them so that you can start preparing for the exams, which also tested our conceptual understanding of the material.

In summary, ME11c was a really fun and interesting course, a bit difficult to grasp at times, and definitely had homeworks that should not be pushed off to the last minute, but by spending time on this class, you can really walk out feeling as though you took a lot from it.

Don't be frightened by ME11c! Coming in to this class, I had heard a lot of negative things about it (both about Ruby as a prof and the difficulty of the material). Don't listen to them! I actually liked Ruby a lot as a prof; from my understanding, the class definitely improved in organization this year, which is likely because Ruby has more experience as a prof, but she's actually really nice and willing to answer questions when you're confused, so don't be afraid to interrupt during lecture or ask her after class. That being said, this is definitely a class you need to pay attention to, or at least read the lecture materials and keep up with. Put in the time to understand the math on the sets (TAs are super helpful and willing to explain!) because this really paid off for me when it came to both building intuition and performing well on the exams otherwise. This felt like a solid introduction to propulsion and fluids in general.
