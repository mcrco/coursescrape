This is a great class to learn engineering principles. The content was super useful for my internships/SURFs. This is the most applicable class I have had so far for ME core.
ME14 has been one of my favorite courses at Caltech. I think this class strikes an excellent balance between understand the theory part of some aspects of Mechanical Engineering and actually applying it. It is in ME core for a good reason.

The course starts off with an introduction to understanding different factors that go into consideration when designing a component or system in a real world setting and taking a more real world approach to understand the concepts of stress and strain that you covered in ME12. You go into much more detail into understanding the true behavior of materials under different loading conditions, which was really cool theory to learn. Professor Mello goes through his own example of understanding how different loading conditions and stresses led to the malfunction of a part in a real industry. Then, at least this year, we delved into fatigue failure. This has to be one of the coolest things I have ever learned, with some of if not the most engaging lectures I have had. While in ME12 you learned just about whether a part will yield/fail under static conditions, you now expand your understanding to see how repeated, cyclic loading can induce a new kind of failure mode. Professor Mello did an excellent job during these lectures introducing the theory and going through the mathematical derivation, and then applying it to real life scenarios. This was a really captivating part of the class. One other note, at least this year, the homeworks took a bit of time, so I would definitely recommend starting as soon as you can.

After these lectures, there are still a couple that cover GD&T. But what happens after midterms week is that you then transition into the project phase of the class. The transmission project is a lot of fun. Of course, between designing, putting together the design review presentations, making edits, and finalizing your design, you will quickly see the amount of time that you put into this class start to increase a bit. This continues when you then start to go into the shop to machine your parts and put it all together. This means spending a couple hours each day during these two weeks in the shop. While it takes time, it is at least fun. My best recommendation is to start machining as soon as you can, plan out when each operation needs to get done by and by whom, and then be careful machining. Also, make sure to include some buffer time in the schedule for debugging and re-machining. Apart from that, enjoy the project!

In summary, ME14 was a really fun class with both cool theory and very useful application, Professor Mello does an outstanding job lecturing and engaging students, and the transmission project is a very unique experience that you can get a lot out of.
The transmission project will be a ton of fun if you do it right. Make sure you have a good team, people who you can work with and have fun at the same time.
Find a good group. It's ideal if you've already worked extensively with your group members before so you have an idea of everyone's contributions.
