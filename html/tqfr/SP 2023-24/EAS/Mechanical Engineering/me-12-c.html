Comments

What would you tell a student thinking about taking this course in the future?

This course is more relaxed than the other ones mechanical engineering students typically take during third term. I would recommend starting earlier on sets. They are not extremely hard, it is just better for time management purposes and because office hours are earlier if you have any questions.
Good class overall. ME 12c seemed to be much more reasonable than ME 12a/b. Prof Asimaki is passionate about teaching and cares about the students. TAs are also great.
ME12c is an interesting class that I think deviates from the rest of the 12 sequence, but is still pretty engaging. It is different in that in Me12a/b, the focus was much more on statics and mechanics of materials. Here, you are now entering the field of dynamics.

The course starts off by looking at velocity and acceleration equations in the planar case before then extending these to 3D space. There are a few equations that you will learn in these first few weeks that will serve as the basis for all the rest of the material in this course, so make sure you know them well, where they apply, and how to use them. They deal mostly with understanding how to relate the velocities and accelerations of points in different frames of reference. This becomes the basis of the first half of the course. After the midterm, there is then a bit more of a shift in the material. You then start to focus much more on rotations, starting off with moments/products of inertia and then expanding with the topic of angular momentum and learning how to use these in problems. The course then takes another shift into using energy/momentum principles, before concluding with vibrations. There were times where some of the material felt like an extension of kinematics from physics. However, there were many more subtleties and complicated cases to understand, particularly as you get to the latter topics, so make sure to spend some time really internalizing the material.

To understand the concepts, honestly, the best practice is to go to lecture. I found these to be particularly useful in keeping me aware about what the class was currently covering and served as a good way to get familiar with the concepts before tackling the homeworks. Professor Asimaki really does care about teaching this material. She is extremely receptive to student feedback and actively makes effort to improve the lectures and the course overall, so I highly recommend going to her lectures. After going to lecture, brushing up with the book would also be good to catch any of the subtleties and small details that you missed in lecture. As for the homeworks themselves, they were very manageable for a 9 unit class. Of course, there were times when you might get confused on some parts (since sometimes dynamics can be pretty unintuitive), so I would not recommend leaving these to the last day. Starting early gives you enough time to think through them and learn from them.

As for exams, at least this year, I found the final to be a bit more difficult than the midterm exam. Both exams are very generous when it comes to timing, and even if you don't get a question completely right, at least putting down the starting equations and doing some reasonable work would earn partial credit, which was nice. If there are any review sessions, I highly recommend attending those!

In short, ME12c was pretty interesting, there were definitely some times when conceptually the small details would not be immediately intuitive, but overall it was a fair workload and the course staff really do their best to help your learning.
ME12C is not particularly difficult to get through but it can be very challenging to properly understand if you are not putting significant effort into the class. The textbook is by far your best friend. Most homework problems come from the textbook and so you can easily find the problem and review the preceding section in the textbook to get an idea the concepts/equations you'll need. Gathering the equations is easy but understanding what they mean is difficult. The second half of the course is not particularly difficult and felt more like a more in depth return to Ph1a. Lectures can be highly variable and not terribly necessary, however they are sometimes useful to stay on track or whenever they go over practice problems. Set's were very fair. The midterm was fair. The final felt completely disjointed, difficult to comprehend, and fairly unfair.
ME12c is pretty chill, honestly. My best description is that it's like AP Physics C (Mechanics) on steroids. If you felt uncomfortable with vector math/notation before, now is a good time to brush up on cross-products. Overall though, use the textbook as an excellent resource and just keep up with the sets and the class won't be too bad.
Asimaki is such a sweet professor and a great person to talk to.

However this class I felt the book helped alot with the example problems and understanding the course content. There was unfortunately a ridiculous disconnection between the head TA and the content of the class in which the final displayed content we were extremely new to and had not been tested on via homework). There also was no curve which was quite surprising given the final's difficult content and the extra credit we were supposed to receive from attending lecture, this was extremely disappointing to me and many of my fellow peers who saw our final grades to be rather curved down in a sense and not at all what we were expecting.
The TAs played an integral role in this course. They had a strong understanding of the concepts and explained things very well. I highly recommend attending office hours. The textbook, "Engineering Mechanics - Dynamics" by Hibbeler, was also an excellent resource. Over the term, lecture attendance declined. This was partly due to the three-hour block of classes (Me12c followed by Me14). Although being in a lecture hall for 3 hours twice a week was pretty tiring, some of us chose not to attend 12c lectures mainly because we found that reading the textbook was more beneficial to our understanding than attending lectures.

As for exams, I think the midterm was pretty fair and covered things that we did in class and on sets. As someone who attended most of the lectures after midterms, I thought that the final exam (which was based on the second half of the class) was difficult. Even during the final review, we were introduced to new applications of formulas derived in class that hadn't been used in problem sets.
