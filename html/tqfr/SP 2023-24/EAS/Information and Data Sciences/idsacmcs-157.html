This is a Zuev class, so you already know it's going to be excellent! Like all of his classes, the lecture notes are exceptional, the sets are well-paced, and the material builds on itself in an approachable manner towards some really interesting topics. (I hear the lectures were really good too, but oops they were at 9 am and I am sleepy.)

I had a somewhat weak background in applied statistics (I'd only taken Ma 3 and not taken ACM/EE/IDS 116), but I don't think this hurt me too much, since the class started from fairly simple basics and built from there. You definitely need solid intuitions for probability and statistics though (or collaboration partners who are able to help point out when you've gone wrong), or it's easy to get misled -- stats is just like that!

The class is organized as six sets, a midterm, and a final. (The midterm and final are basically like the sets, just no-collab and limited time.) The class expects you to be able to write Matlab, but it's not a lot of code, so I think it's approachable from a CS 1 background (or, if you're particularly ambitious, even without a CS background at all -- although they're making CS 1 part of Core, right? so I guess all of you have a CS background now...). Like most Zuev classes, it's run through Piazza and you can get extra credit by answering other students' questions there, although I never did. There isn't really a textbook.

For my fellow CNS majors: There are probably easier ways to fulfill the CNS requirement than this class, but this was a really *interesting* way to fulfill it, and I don't regret my choice in the slightest. Definitely make sure you've done Ma 3 first, and if you've already done ACM 95a with Zuev, then you should have a pretty good sense of what to expect from this class as well.

Overall, I learned a lot about stats and would highly recommend this class to anyone who's interested in how to perform effective statistics, the mathematical foundations of them, and how to avoid common pitfalls!
