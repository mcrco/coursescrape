The class was criticized for being too difficult in previous years, but I feel like this year it swung back to being easier than expected. This difficulty balanced out by the end of the course though, and I think it was at the correct level of difficulty.

His lecture notes are very helpful, and the Spivak textbook tends to be a good source of examples and more intuitions for his explanations. The professor tends to like purely abstract definitions (which is to be expected), but it helps to gain a more geometric intuition as well.

Overall, highly recommend to anyone who likes pure math. The course gives off good vibes, the grading is fair, and you can tell other people that you took the analytical track and gain their sympathy.
I think this course is best suited for those who already have a good background in multivariable calculus and a little bit of an introduction to differential forms and differential geometry already. For the most part the course problem sets aren't very difficult, but learning the content from this course is not easy, so I recommend having already introduced yourself to the content.
The class is definitely easier than the previous iterations described. Tony definitely was focused on the learning aspect of the class, and the sets definitely assisted in such learning. The material is still quite dense, as we cover most of Spivak (everything but chapter 4, integration on chains), but the sets definitely posit intriguing questions to assist learning. Overall, I am glad I took the class as opposed to the practical track.
I love this class!!!! Go to office hours and don't be afraid to ask questions (even super basic ones). Lectures are basically the lecture notes. you learn a lot and are able to understnad easily through sets. office hours!!
The grades at the end of the course are curved so you aren't disadvantaged versus the practical track so the percentages of your homeworks and quizzes aren't reflective of your final grade. If you really want to understand the material, you have to devote quite a lot of time outside class reading through the references and other textbooks, as well as asking for help. But again, grades are curved so don't worry too much about failing the course.
Ensure to attend every single class and take notes.
Don't worry about this course! The professor actually listens to student feedback and will always aim to make the course as fair and manageable as possible. I highly recommend this course for students who wish to have a more rigorous approach to multivariate and vector calculus.
This class was pretty good. I heard from many upperclassman that ma1c is extremely hard and not worth the effort. However, the difficulty of this class has been significantly reduced from past years. Essentially, the prof just took the hardest 1 or 2 questions off of each homework set (more specifically, they became "bonus" problems, but they aren't worth any points). In addition, the midterm and final mostly consisted off computation rather than proofs. Of course, the material itself is somewhat challenging to digest, but the course is quite doable now. If you're interested in mathematics and understanding the theoretical underpinnings of multivariable calculus, take this class! Plus, you get to learn about differential forms, which are pretty cool in my opinion (even as a non-math-major). I would recommend going to lecture, but you can also get by decently well using the lecture notes (which, while concise, do in fact cover everything covered in lecture). The homework sets usually only take 3-5 hours. Towards the end of the class, I would also recommend skimming through "A Visual Introduction to Differential Forms and Calculus on Manifolds" to get a more intuitive understanding of differential geometry (trust me, it's not as hard as it seems). In conclusion, this class has more a "prac" feel than before, but is still quite theoretical and interesting. You should take it!
