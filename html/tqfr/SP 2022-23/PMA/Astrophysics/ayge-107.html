This was my favorite class at Caltech so far. In the small telescopes portion, you get to set up and use two telescopes for observation on the Cahill roof, which is great, especially if you've never had observation experience. This takes about four Thursday night sessions. The field trip to Palomar was amazing and genuinely a once-in-a-lifetime experience if you're not pursuing astronomy in the future. The monastery is lovely and cozy, the nature is beautiful, and the observatory is amazing.

However, this class is a significant amount of work. The Palomar project runs throughout the entire term, and there's an assignment (basically a very, very long Jupyter notebook) due every other week. Each assignment took me the equivalent of maybe one CS 24 assignment. I still think the lab portions made it worth it, but make sure you have enough time in your schedule to work on these assignments.
This was a really memorable and fun class, but also a ton of work. As a CS major with very little background in astronomy, I found the assignments (especially the imaging and spectroscopy tutorial) to be really time-consuming, maybe about the same difficulty as a CS2 assignment. Grading can get pretty harsh, and the directions/background on homework isn't very clear at times so go to office hours if you're stuck!

Besides that though, I'd still recommend this class to anyone for the small telescope observation nights the Cahill roof and the fun fieldtrip to Palomar Observatory. The instructors were also super chill and passionate about the subject, and receptive to feedback as well. This was only the second time the course had been offered, so I'm sure it'll only improve moving forward.
Amazing class! The material is really interesting and is taught incredibly well! The assignments do tend to be quite long, but Zac was a wonderful resource and was always available to provide assistance. Fair warning- there's a lot of coding expected, all of which is in python. Make sure you have a decent enough foundation with numpy and matplotlib, and expect to spend a loooot of time looking at online documentation. I took this class as a non-Ay major, which was the majority of the class (3/10 students were Ay). The instructors were both very conscious of this when presenting material, so it is paced to give everyone the necessary background in imaging regardless of astro experience. The Palomar trip was also insanely fun, and definitely made the class really unique. My only small complaint about the course would be timing/workload, as the palomar project + long assignments + small telescope observing nights/assignment definitely provides a lot to fit into 10 weeks, making this course a bit underunited, with a somewhat substantial assignment due each week. However, only 2-3 of these assignments are truly terribly long, and all of the assignments are quite interesting so it wasn't really a massive drawback personally. Overall, if you have the opportunity to take this class and are at all interested, I 100% suggest taking it, it's an incredible experience.
This is definitely my favorite course out of all the courses I've taken at Caltech so far -- it is one of the most rewarding ones and what I feel like Caltech tries to advertise most of its courses as. Of course, the highlight of the course was going to Palomar and having the opportunity to use the 200-inch telescope for a project of our choice. I could probably write an entire essay on why this was such an enriching experience, but to keep it short, seeing the images of our astronomical object appear on the screen for the first time was inspiring. The professors and TA were also very kind and during the trip we all got to know them better -- not many classes here offer a chance to interact with professors and TAs outside of the course in that way.

Prior to the trip, we spent a lot of time working on a project proposal and creating a detailed observing plan; this may have taken me longer than other people with more experience in this field, as I have very limited astrophysics background. The thing that set this course apart from others is that after spending so much time making all these theoretical calculations, we actually got to use it and see it in practice by producing images of objects so far away in the sky. By connecting theory with practice, I felt like I was able to solidify my understanding of these concepts.

By the end of the course, we reduced and analyzed our data and wrote up a final report which was essentially a paper detailing the process and findings of our chosen project (which we also completely designed on our own). Professor Hillenbrand was extremely kind and met up with my group several times to help us on a portion of our project. I never thought I would be able to produce something like this given my minimal background in astrophysics, but this has been such a rewarding process and I've learned so much.

Another component of the course was small telescopes observation nights on the roof of Cahill, which we had around 4 times this term. We got to set up and operate these telescopes ourselves and use them to make observations. It was an extremely accessible way, again, to put things we learned in lecture to practice. We also had four other assignments to learn about topics such as image processing, spectroscopic data processing, and databases. These were pretty coding heavy, as was the Palomar project analysis (since you either take images or do spectroscopy on an object), so I think it would be helpful, if not necessary, to be comfortable with Python. Some assignments, especially the imaging and spectroscopy assignments, took significantly longer (~20 hours) than others. However, I think all the time I spent on these assignments was meaningful as I learned something every step of the way.

Whether you are taking this class for a requirement or are considering taking this class out of interest, I would highly recommend it. It definitely requires some time and effort, but is one of the most memorable experiences I've had here. :)
