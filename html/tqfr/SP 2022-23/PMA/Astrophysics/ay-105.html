Do you have any constructive comments for other students considering taking this course? In particular, comments about workload/distribution of the workload of the course, the necessity of the textbook, unexpected time requirements or flexibility, or unspecified prerequisites could be particularly helpful.

The material that this lab class offers is honestly some of the most interesting that I have encountered during my time at this school. However the class suffered from major organizational issue that made the class feel much more like a chore since concepts were often not well explained and there was often a disconnect between what was expected to do in lab and what was instructed to do in lab. In fairness to the instructor, this was his first year teaching it, so I expect for these issues to be alleviated the next time it is offered, so I would still recommend people to try the class out since the material for the class is so interesting.
The material of this class is very interesting, but it was frustrating that the course instructors often did not know how to conduct the experiments and thus were unable to help troubleshoot when the students reached a problem and had a middling understanding of the science behind the experiments at best. Much of our time in lab was wasted gathered around an instructor trying to find some software feature on the one lab laptop that simply didn't exist. Often times, the professor would just leave the lab room for hours in the middle of the three hour lab sessions. I understand that this is the first year that this prof has taught this course since the material was updated but I would expect more from a working astrophysicist.

The weekly, entirely handwritten lab reports are supposed to take 3 hours a week outside of lab (6 hours lab + 3 hours outside = 9 units), which the instructors consistently stressed to us. However, every single student in the class spent at least 4-6 hours on the lab reports, with most spending 7-9 hours per week. The professor and TA snidely remarking on how short and easy the reports were supposed to be certainly did not inspire confidence. Part of this came from the harsh grading scheme for the labs. Points were deducted for esoteric infractions, like not handwriting your discussion and conclusion or not including serial numbers for every part (even posts and post holders ??). It was also difficult to know what was expected for the reports since the expectations were constantly changing. We often wouldn't get feedback for lab reports until we had already submitted later reports, meaning we'd get points deducted for the same unmet expectations twice since we didn't know that those expectations existed. Grace for late work was also non-existent (harsh policy and extensions rarely granted), since it would "inconvenience the TA" by handing in work even a few minutes late. I refer to my earlier comment that grades would not be returned often for over a week after submitting the work.

TLDR I chose to be an astro major in part to get out of taking Ph 6 and 7. Had I known Ay 105 was going to be like this, I would've just been a physics major.
If you have any other option, do not take this class unless you are sure that it will be with a different instructor and TA. Among other things, it was severely underunited and the grading was extremely harsh and we were marked off a letter grade for very petty things that would not concern an actual lab scientist in the slightest. Sometimes, I would spend well over 10 hours on the lab writeup (it was nominally supposed to be max three) and got a C or a D for some really weird reasons. In fact, I'm pretty sure it would have been possible to get negative grades if Emily used her grading scheme (which she, incidentally, refused to give a full description of) consistently. This made the class very stressful and significant portions of lab time were often spent frantically trying to find the part numbers for every last component, including things like posts and post holders and stands that didn't have clear labels. This is probably partly because the TA did not seem to know what the standard expectations are for a lab notebook. Also, there were only one to two experimental setups (depending on the experiment) and about 10 people in the class, so the groups were often not very cohesive (with some students dominating the lab work to an unreasonable degree). The lab was also very poorly organized and a lot of time was wasted sitting around (even for e.g. over half of the lab time). Also, despite working with infrared lasers and beam splitters there were no safety glasses in the lab. One of the students raised this concern to Professor Hallinan, and he was dismissive of it. More generally, neither he nor the TA gave the sense that they would be receptive to feedback, there was no ombudsperson, and although we (the students) all discussed our collective concerns amongst ourselves, nothing ended up being done about it. Besides all of this, the TA and Professor Hallinan were basically never prepared for lab, didn't actually know how to do the experiments, were sometimes even unfamiliar with the equipment, and often gave confusing or no answers to questions. If you want to learn about optics instrumentation, take the optics track of Ph 77 instead. This class was a miserable experience, I very much regret taking it. In fact, it was even more unpleasant than Ph 106 bc in my book (and I didn't like that class at all), because at least in Ph 106 bc I felt like I was learning things and that my time wasn't being wasted.
This course is a decent amount of work, with the lab write-ups taking much longer than 3 hours per week. I also found the hand written notebook requirement as unnecessary, making the write-ups much more time consuming than they needed to be. The labs were all fairly engaging, although the organization could use a bit of work
