Good course
In many ways, the work of a physicist is easy. We risk very little, yet enjoy a position of gratification over those who offer up their souls, their dignity, and their order of magnitude estimates to our judgment in attempt to make understanding physics more straightforward and timely. But the bitter truth we physicists or physics enthusiasts must face, is that in the grand scheme of things, the average masterwork of "this makes sense, I think?" art you turn in as your Ph101 set after several long nights of working in Linde Hall probably requires more precision than you would expect from an order of magnitude class. But there are times when a physicist truly risks something, and that is in the discovery and defense of the *new*. The new demands constant brain cell donations to the cause of striving to accrete enough brain mass to remember physics fundamentals including but not limited to quantum mechanics and electricity & magnetism, as well as hidden pre-requisites that you actually need to understand mechanical engineering concepts pertaining to solid state physics (especially thermodynamics and working with stress/strain under bulk moduli) to course through the formidable problem sets of this class.

This morning, I experienced something new: an extraordinary final exam from a singularly unexpected seed of concepts I realized I did not have a sufficiently seasoned background in to finish within the time limit of 4 hours. To say that both the course of Ph101 and its creator, Professor Sterl Phinney, have challenged my preconceptions about physics being something I could possibly pursue as a research career is a gross understatement. They have rocked me to my core. In the past, I have made no secret of my dread for Caltech students' famous motto, "Caltech: where your best isn't good enough." But I realize, only now do I truly understand what they meant. Not everyone can become a great physicist; but a great physicist *can* come from grad school. It is difficult to imagine more humble origins than those of the geniuses capable of doing physics at Sterl's behest, who are, in my opinion, nothing less than the finest physicists at Caltech. I will be returning to similar physics courses soon, hungry for more but with the wisdom that I really should have taken this class on pass-fail.

I took this class because it has a reputation of "being one of the best classes at Caltech" or at the very least, a "must-take" class. I would say this class is quite difficult, but in the end, it is rewarding. I learned a lot over the course of this class and got more comfortable with making estimates and thinking through and approaching problems in a very different way than I am used to in other physics classes. Despite finding some of the HW problems to be quite unpleasant to complete and the final to be quite difficult due to the time pressure, this class was worth taking and I am glad I took it. The way of thinking emphasized in this class doesn't come naturally to me and I did get better at it through this course.

The HWs take a long time to complete and are not easy. Office hours are pretty helpful when you are stuck on the HW but you often get vague hints and have to spend a lot of time to decipher them and figure the problem out. The grading on the HWs is not very lenient and since there was no clear rubric, it felt like an arbitrary amount of points was being deducted for minor errors. There is a huge amount of time pressure in the final exam, I think the final was graded a little more leniently than HWs to account for this.

The lectures are the most important part of this class and if you want to do well, you should attend the lectures (there are no notes posted and a lot of the HW questions rely on using similar techniques to the ones used in class). Prof. Phinney is very knowledgeable and does a decent job of explaining various concepts. One of my biggest gripes with this class is that the lectures were just a little too fast-paced. Had they been 15% slower, I think I would have grasped more material and gotten more out of the class - it always felt like I was in a rush to copy everything on the board but that meant not always having the time to focus on what Prof. Phinney is saying when he is explaining his approach/reasoning to problems that we discussed in the class.

Overall, I think if you take this class on grades and put in the work, you will learn a lot from the class but it will not be a very fun journey (due to nature of the HWs and the final). So consider taking it on pass-fail if you are interested in the class but want lower stakes (which might allow you to enjoy the class more if you aren't as stressed about needing to do well in it).
