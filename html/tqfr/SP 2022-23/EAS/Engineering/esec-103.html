Comments

Do you have any constructive comments for other students considering taking this course? In particular, comments about workload/distribution of the workload of the course, the necessity of the textbook, unexpected time requirements or flexibility, or unspecified prerequisites could be particularly helpful.

This was a fantastic course, probably the most useful/applicable course to the "real-world" that I will ever take at Caltech. You will get out of this course what you put into it. If your team is solid and you pick a topic interesting to you, you will be amazed how much you learn about and do over the course of 9 weeks. We start with trying to choose an emerging technology and have lectures based on a textbook by Oliver deWeck. Over the course of the term, the lectures are sometimes cancelled to give us time to work on the project, as it requires talking to 10+ outside experts to give us info on how to "predict" the future evolution of a field/technology using the techniques in the book. Even though the course description mentions doing Business School case studies, we never did.
The start of the course is pretty relaxed, with reading and some minor analysis of data.
Coming up to the midterms increases the workload slightly, as projects are still establishing what they actually want to work on. After the midterms the projects pick up pace, with a lot of expert interviews to be done between then and the finals. Finals are a lot of work, to synthesize all the information from experts and literature into a coherent proposal.
This class was fantastic. If it is taught by Richard, you can expect above average same day communications and attention to detail. The class was a combination of reading, lecture, guest lecture, and industry communication. Go in to this class ready to dive into an unfamiliar topic of your choice guided by your interests, industry knowledge and a cadre of experts in technology management.
Come in and find people you like working with, and what you get out is what you put in
Useful for later stage undergrads/grad students, especially those who want critical feedback on real startups they'd like to pursue.
The class is very collaborative and engaging. It is structured as a quarter long market/technology research project. Groups are formed early and are composed of 3-5 people. Be prepared to present & practice public speaking frequently.
This is quite possibly the most unique and lifechanging course I've taken at Caltech. It taught me how to think about the world in a different way than the rest of my classes - as a problem solver rather than a scientist.
I was not a big fan of the textbook and didn't think it added a lot to the class. The guest lectures were interesting, but the best part was learning from Richard, Stu, and Eric's experiences and hearing their feedback on our projects.
Although, at times, it felt unclear what the course objectives were, the overall sense was that if you put in effort you would do well overall.
