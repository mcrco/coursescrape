Assignment 4 took me longer than all the previous assignments combined (it was all I worked on for 2 weeks, and I barely finished) and assumed a high level of comfort with probability. Since the assignment was due 2 weeks after drop day, those of us on grades had no choice but to devote all our efforts to it. Furthermore, answers to parts of assignment 4 were basically given away at office hours, and I think the difficulty of the assignment actually caused us to learn much less, as we were focused on just getting through it. The project proposal (a 4-page paper) was due 2 days after assignment 4.
- Grading was very variable. There were a lot of ambiguous questions, and some TAs graded more harshly than others. No grades were released at all until a couple of days before drop day!
- Students were treated very disrespectfully. This class was theoretically co-taught by Perona and Georgia, but in practice Perona only gave a couple lectures while Georgia was much more active. Unfortunately, Georgia was very rude to students and constantly made excuses / dismissed concerns. This attitude was mirrored by some of the TAs. Students were shamed for asking questions, and constantly told to drop the class. There were multiple instances in office hours where I witnessed other students ask clarifying questions and the TA responded very defensively, accusing them of not wanting to put in effort. There were similar instances on Piazza -- to give just one of several examples, a student posted a very reasonable question, and explained why they were still confused after reading the paper for that assignment. The instructor response was just "read the paper."
- All in all, I found it ironic that this class had an unusually harsh late policy and grading policy, while the professors and TAs were themselves extremely late with grading and reluctant to help students.

For next year, they are planning to introduce hard prerequisites for this class. I'm not sure this is the right move, as I think the class would have been relatively doable if the assignments had fewer mistakes, and more qualified students will probably experience a worse learning-to-tediousness ratio. However, I think if they follow through on getting more TAs it's possible the class will be a much better experience next year.

This is one of my favorite courses I have taken, and covers much of the state-of-the-art in modern ML, including transformers and diffusion models. The lectures from Georgia are excellent and highly informative, and the guest lectures include high-profile ML researchers. The assignments are long and definitely require the two weeks over which they are due, but are doable within a reasonable amount of time. Each week there was a paper reading assignment where we would choose a paper from a given list and write our comments about them on Piazza. There is also a final project proposal at the end, where you write up your own idea for an ML paper - although you do not implement it.

I do not recommend taking this course before you have taken CS 155 and ACM 116, or have equivalent background/experience. These are very strong prerequisites and the material will be significantly more difficult without them.
