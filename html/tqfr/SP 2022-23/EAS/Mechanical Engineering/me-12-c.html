Example problems were the best way to learn. Also other textbooks on dynamics help as different textbooks have different ways of explaining the topics, so finding one that clicks with you is really helpful. I recommend attending lecture and staying engaged in the course.
This class is much harder than 12 a and b. Somewhat builds on the material, but it is also very different and requires more conceptual thinking.
I don't think I can genuinely say that I understand or learned a thing in this class, didn't seem like the prof made a legitimate effort to improve the way lectures were structured so that students were learning the content (or learning a progression of different concepts for that matter) or about making them comprehendible either.

We asked for examples, or images, or literally anything that wasn't just random potatoes on the board with vectors sticking out. We could have referenced neat example problems from the textbook on the projector during lecture or videos of how things looked so we could visualize them so we weren't wasting the class period trying to decipher the what was written/drawn or trying to figure out what the problem actually was asking to begin with.

It also would have been helpful to have explicitly written out definitions, concepts, equations, all outlined for us so we could actually understand what was going on in a chronological way (sentences!). If we were taught how exactly to approach problem types clearly that would have also helped. Also would have been helpful to have legible course notes (that aren't just 2 pages with random-looking doodles and miscellaneous equations, with textbook chapter references we can't trace down, (which book, which edition, no one I know actually uses the same text book or addition for this class)) earlier on in the term and to have them released before class so we could follow along with the lectures.

I feel like the junior TAs taught this class, not the prof, and going to lecture generally confused my understanding of how we were supposed to approach problems.

Also it would have been helpful to have had solutions posted not just a few days before the final so we could review for the midterm. On that note, we basically had no midterm review, and essentially no final review, and then the prof left the country two weeks ago and dumped a 5 hour final with 4 questions on us to do within 2 days. RIP

Overall, this was not a class, it was a DIY course entailing: "watch youtube and maybe a miscellaneous textbook will help you", certainly not up to Caltech standards.
The students understand that this is a hard course to teach, it's also much harder to learn, and we were generally left stranded in the middle of an ocean of random equations with terms we didn't fully understand.

Lastly, in terms of grading, it is actually unfair to have specific TAs assigned to specific people's full sets. The TAs grade notably different/harsher than others, to the point where you can literally tell who is grading your set (confirmed by hw correction notes). Depending on whos set is assigned to which TA, people get unfair grades compared to peers. Rather, specific questions should be assigned to TAs like in other classes.
