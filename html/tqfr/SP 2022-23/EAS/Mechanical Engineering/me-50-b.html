Comments

Do you have any constructive comments for other students considering taking this course? In particular, comments about workload/distribution of the workload of the course, the necessity of the textbook, unexpected time requirements or flexibility, or unspecified prerequisites could be particularly helpful.

Prof. Mello has a great enthusiasm for teaching that makes the class enjoyable regardless of personal interest. A lighter workload this term also left more room to take the time to better understand the material. I did feel that less time could have been safely spent on some material in order to cover more ground, but I liked the amount of space allowed by the format this year.
Nice intro to numerical and experimental fluid mechanics. From my experience, the course is substantially back-loaded. Coding assignments are assigned once per week and take a couple hours but increase in complexity. The final project (combined PIV and ANSYS analysis) takes pretty long but you have two weeks to do it
ME50b was a breath of fresh air from the insane workload of ME50a. As opposed to 4 long individual reports, 50b had only 1 (ONE!!!) report, done as a group. The group aspect of the report was a bit of a mess as each group comprised of 6-7 people, but it did greatly lessen the workload to a very reasonable amount. Throughout the term you also have a couple matlab and ANSYS assignments that aren't more than a couple hours each (some could probably be done in under 1 if you know what you're doing).

As for lectures, Prof. Mello puts a lot of care into explaining fluids concepts really well.
Prof. Mello's passion is palpable. He is an excellent guide through the world of experimental fluid mechanics.
ME 50b was different this year compared to how it has been done in the past. The topics were flipped around, with 50a being solids and 50b being fluids. The idea was that the material taught during the solids portion would be more applicable to ME 72ab. Interesting thought, but I'm not sure if it really worked out that way. In addition, instead of submitting two separate reports (one on PIV and one on ANSYS), they were combined into one report that compared experimental and numerical results. Furthermore, this was a group assignment so you were able to work on it with up to 5 other people. This was certainly a lot of work, but if you have a good group it shouldn't be any problem. There were also smaller Matlab assignments during the first half of the term. These were not done in groups, but were not very challenging as most of the code was provided. The results were certainly interesting though. I really enjoyed 50b, Professor Mello's passion is truly contagious. I also really liked how the assignments had tangible results. You could see the figures you produced and if done correctly, they look pretty cool.
I feared fluids going into this course, but it not only taught me fluid dynamics, but it did so in a way that wasn't miserable. Compared to Me 11c, it is much more visual, which personally helped me.
There was only one group/double-length report about the one lab and the ANSYS analysis of the corresponding simulation.
Took quite a different approach than 50A. One single lab report, which was a group project, that covered both the Ansys simulation and the water tunnel experiment in the lab. A few other random assignments, mostly tutorials that walked you through step by step.
