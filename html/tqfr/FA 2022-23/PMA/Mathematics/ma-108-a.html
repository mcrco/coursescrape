Okay so I have mixed feelings about 108a this year. Basically, we focused a lot on the metric space topology stuff, so ideas like compactness, completeness, and continuous mappings were very central to like 80% of the course. This is in contrast to how the material is presented in Rudin (our textbook) and how I imagine it is usually presented, where these ideas are mostly a short prelude to the meat of a first real analysis course in differentiation and integration. Then, randomly at the end, we start looking at basic functional analysis and fourier analysis. So it definitely was not what I expected to learn.

However, I think I liked this way more than I would've liked the usual way, so I am actually thankful we focused mostly on what we did (although I still think the fourier stuff was out of left field). I took Ma 109a concurrently with 108a, and I think they interacted in a really interesting way that helped me get a lot more out of 108a than I think I could've without 109a. Additionally, the topics that we missed (differentiation and integration) are covered pretty heavily in Ma 1a, which I understand most people do not really learn much from, but I felt like I got enough from that class to get the gist of a more analysis-based rigorous definition of things like limits, derivatives, and riemann integrals. So yeah, if the class continues to be taught in this style, I wouldn't mind it, but it definitely is untraditional from what I've heard, also taking into account the people I've talked to who took real analysis at other schools.

Finally, as far as grading and stuff, the class was pretty nice. The TAs were very fair and not out to get you at all, and the final curve was pretty generous considering that I think the average grade was pretty high.

I am a graduate student in mechanical engineering, and this course was the hardest course I have ever taken. I had never done a formal math proof before, and every lecture and assignment is proofs. For non-math majors, if you want to challenge yourself and truly learn how to do proofs, this is a good class, but very difficult. I left each lecture with no idea what I had just written down, and I need significant self-study to follow along in the class. I watched every real analysis lecture by Francis Su on Youtube in which he provides significant intuition that was not given in the Caltech version of the course. You can also buy the paperback version of Rudin for pretty cheap. Rudin lacks insight, so if you are a non-math major I might look for a more recent real analysis book with more intuition. If you decide to take this course, if I could pass it with no math proof history, you can too. Good luck
