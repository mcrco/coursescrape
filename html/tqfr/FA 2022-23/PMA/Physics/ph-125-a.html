porter lectures at a very high level. material this year was very math/group theory/representation theory based, not really what I was expecting or looking for. I found that the course was fairly straightforward until the midterm, but that the material got significantly more difficult as we moved into group theory and spin-related content. I've never taken group theory myself, but it is my understanding that people who were taking a group theory course found that helpful in some aspects of this course.
For students taking this class, I recommend taking plenty of mathematical physics classes prior to this. It relied very heavily on Group Theory, which I have no background in. My mathematical/physical intuition is not very great so this class was a struggle. For my peers who concomitantly took Ph129, I think they may have had a better time connecting the dots of the course's concepts, whereas to me, they were foreign and abstract.
I found the course content of Ph125a very interesting; I think I learned a lot from just doing the problems on the sets, which were very well-designed and gave us broader insight into the applications of what we were learning. Professor Porter focused very strongly on the mathematical foundation of quantum mechanics, and group theory, which in some cases I think was too far removed from the topic we were trying to understand. As a result, lectures were pretty dry. Taking Ph129a concurrently with this class was very useful, and things will be easier if you have a background in group theory. We often went into more mathematical depth than the textbook, and for the second half of the course the textbook didn't end up being that useful-- we went through the typical quantum mechanics content fairly quickly and spent a lot of time proving things in group theory. The exams followed about the same format as the sets, which was unfortunate since there was often an element of understanding we needed for the sets beyond just what was taught in class, for which I'd go to office hours or need a lot more time to think about. Overall, I would say this was a very interesting but challenging class. I would recommend using Professor Porter's group theory and angular momentum notes for the second half of the sets and the textbook for the first half.
This class started out not that hard, but became pretty difficult after the midterm. Background in group theory is basically required, as the second half of this class is entirely that, but it is taught very poorly and a thorough grasp is expected. The final was brutal.
Overall, I had a good time and learned a lot. I do have a few points of interest for anyone considering taking it, though. Firstly, there is a lot of math in this class. Weeks 4-9 were almost entirely math, which was not stated at the start (it diverged from the syllabus pretty hard around midterms). Furthermore, the math is something you likely haven't seen before and has a very high barrier of entry (namely, Lie Algebras, Lie Groups, representation theory). Professor Porter does a pretty good job of explaining it initially, but I definitely hit a wall towards the end of term that required lots of extra reading to feel somewhat comfortable with the material.

I will note, though, that the start of term felt very easy and light. It was probably a 5 unit class until Week 5ish when it very suddenly became a 15-20 unit class.

Now, the grading scheme is very skewed, which feels almost required given the material (70 HW/ 10 midterm/ 20 final). The midterm and final were both pretty tricky. I felt that each of them were mostly fair except for one question that felt pretty unrelated to the class.

Professor Porter does seem to care very much about our learning, though. He stayed over an hour late for office hours to help us out and tries to make things as understandable as possible.
For some reason, this course had a primary focus on group theory that was not well communicated at the beginning of the class. It seemed like the course content of this course was not actually for this course but was taken from an advanced quantum mechanics class. I do not recommend this course for people that do not have a solid quantum mechanics background (this should not be your first or second quantum mechanics class) or else you will be struggling to understand group theory and missing out on actual physics concepts that are important to the course.
