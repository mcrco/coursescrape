Do you have any constructive comments for other students considering taking this course? In particular, comments about workload/distribution of the workload of the course, the necessity of the textbook, unexpected time requirements or flexibility, or unspecified prerequisites could be particularly helpful.

This course has a wealth of interesting content for AMO applications, but may be tough to approach for someone without an AMO background, and even more so for someone without a QM background. This course is slightly tougher than usual as there are quite a number of homework questions that require familiarity with the concepts and calculations involved. You would often have to scour other textbooks and/or notes to guide you through the homework questions. As such, expect to take a bit of time to work on the homework. Nick's notes are constructed to be succinct and pedagogically friendly, but may not include a thorough or rigorous set of instructions/derivations for a complete understanding of each topic. That said, Nick is very approachable so do clarify any doubts you might have and supplement your knowledge as you see fit.
The workload is pretty appropriate. The homework is not too bad and a lot of cool applications of what we learned in class. It is also very relevant to the research happening in a lot of labs on campus which was really useful. You do need a strong background in quantum mechanics, specifically in understanding rules about angular momenta.
This is a great course to take if you are interested in atomic physics! It provides a solid foundation for doing research in the field or if you are just interested in it. The homeworks are very reasonable, and Prof. Hutzler is a great lecturer.
Well-taught! We covered the theories behind many AMO experimental techniques. Be sure to ask a lot of questions because there are so many practical details that go behind these techniques.
Great course if interested in AMO concepts.
good
Course started out fine but the workload increased significantly towards the end of the class. Attending office hours was very helpful for completing the sets in this latter half. The final is a fairly low-stakes presentation on an academic paper, which is a great way to apply the topics you've learned in the class without the stress of a final exam.
This is a great introduction to the world of AMO physics! The course covers a wide range of topics, starting from the fundamentals with atomic energy level structure and eventually covering cool, newer concepts such as laser cooling, different ways of trapping atoms, and even some of the newest techniques in the field of AMO physics. Prof. Hutzler is very passionate not just about the material but also about the quality of the learning experience for the students. The lectures are great, explaining highly complex concept in a systematic and clear manner. The HWs are reasonable for the most part, although a small minority of them have some challenging problems that take longer to solve. Even then, all of the HW problems were very pedagogical and lead to a significant consolidation of the course material. The TAs were very helpful in clarifying conceptual questions about the material and providing support for the HWs.

This class is 80% HW and 20% group presentation during the last week. An entire HW worth of points is considered as a drop set (which can be distributed over different HWs). This makes this class easy to do well in, as long as one keeps up with the material and attends office hours to obtain support and clarification for the HW when needed. The final presentation is done in groups of three and we choose a technique in AMO physics related to but not covered in class to present. This was a great experience in furthering my knowledge about some of the more recent work in the field and was cool to learn about.
