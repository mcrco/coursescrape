Ae 105a is interesting, but it is also challenging. Although there is no midterm or final and problem sets are only due twice a week, they are nothing to scoff at. All of the sets are done in Matlab and the number of problems usually ranges from 3-5, with one or two of them being on the easier side and the rest being quite involved. That being said, the problem sets are very engaging and it is a very rewarding feeling once they are complete. One of the things I liked most about this class is how applicable it is. In the homeworks, you are using really trajectory data, calculating real orbits, and everything feels really applied. This cannot be said with many other Caltech courses and for me, it was very refreshing. Professor Campagnola has immense knowledge about orbital mechanics and astrodynamics. This is very clear from the amount of material presented throughout the term (which I found to be a little too much at times). There were many new, complex topics introduced which sometimes made it hard to digest it all, but I think he knew this and did it on purpose. It seems like part of this course is the introduction of lots of topics so that if you ever encounter them again you will at least have some experience. And given how widely applicable some of the concepts are, far beyond the scope of the course, the chances of that happening are relatively high. As a whole, I definitely enjoyed the class and really liked the format, but I would not recommend taking it unless you have at least some interest in the material. I think there are probably easier classes out there.
This class is quite hard. Definitely one of the hardest I have taken so far. The structure is one set every two weeks and 4-5 total with no exams. The grade being 100% sets it good, but also puts a lot of pressure on you to do these very difficult sets. A STRONG matlab background is recommended as all the sets are fully in matlab. Make use of office hours.
The course is intense (don't start the sets late, just don't) but it is a must-take course for anybody interested in rocket science, highly recommended.
This is essentially a course in orbital mechanics and trajectory planning, and was one of the more practical and hands-on courses I've taken at Caltech, which was refreshing despite the work load. There are five sets, all entirely based in Matlab, assigned roughly every two weeks. Start early, and ask for help/clarifications throughout! A large portion of your time can be spent searching for tiny bugs, so collaboration is also recommended. Prof. Campagnola provides a ton of fascinating anecdotes from his career, and lectures are important to attend as course notes are difficult to decipher. He also gives pointers on the homework. However, I mostly relied on outside resources for figuring out the sets; finding the right website or paper was often key to understanding how to approach a problem.
