This is a really well taught course. There were only 4 sets this term, and each set took roughly 15 hours. The lecture notes aren't great, but that doesn't matter because Jay Polk is an engaging lecturer, who gets through material at a good pace while also pausing often enough to make sure students are still following along. Having a solid background in fluid mechanics and statistical mechanics, is useful, but not necessary.
Really great class if you are interested in rocket propulsion! This class covers propulsion basics (thrust, impulse, power, etc), some thermo, nozzle flow (quasi 1-D compressible flow, supersonic flow, shocks, etc), and then some combustion at the end. The problem sets are very reasonable and some problems are honestly fun to do.

Great class, 10/10
If you are coming from an undergraduate background in mechanical engineering, you will probably already know most of the material in this course. The first 2 or 3 weeks of this course are about rocket performance parameters, why we stage rockets, and equations of motion for rockets. The rest of the course is a review of topics like compressible flow, combustion chemistry, thermodynamics, shock waves, and Laval nozzles that you might have already seen in other classes. If you have previously taken classes on this material, you might find this course repetitive.

There were 4 problem sets this term. They were released about two and a half weeks apart and we usually had about a week and a half to do them. Many questions involve tedious numerical calculations. The problems on compressible flow and shock waves assume a more detailed understanding of the material than what the lectures give you so I would suggest referencing a textbook on compressible flow if you have not taken a class on it already.
