Do you have any constructive comments for other students considering taking this course? I enjoyed this class a lot. Sometimes the lectures were confusing, but there are Jupyter notebooks for each section that are helpful. The homework involves examples of mission design and was not very difficult if you have a good conceptual understanding of the topics. There were a number of guest lecturers from JPL that presented on the applications of the course material.
The course covers a variety of remote sensing techniques. In particular, with increasing wavelength throughout the quarter. There are a good number of guest lecturers from JPL which are fairly interesting. There are 4 sets and a final (basically a no-collaboration set). The textbook is not necessary.
Overall average course at Caltech. Paul as a lecturer is pretty good, but sometimes the course content seems a bit all over the place. The guest lecturers are a huge plus, but I feel this class doesn't teach the theory that well and I never felt prepared for the sets at all.
This class has a lot of potential, but unfortunately it's just really hard to sit through. The lectures consist of the instructor reading off of Jupyter notebooks. As a result, lectures were dreary and it was hard to sift through what was important and what was not. If the instructor used slides to highlight the most important concepts or, even better, the whiteboard, this class would be SO much better. While I still learned some things, it was just really hard to be motivated or excited about the material.

The guest lectures from various JPL scientists, on the other hand, were incredible and made the class worth sticking around for. I especially appreciated David Thompson's and Mike Ressler's lectures. Regarding the latter, I would love to see an in-depth presentation on the technical side of the JWST.

The workload tends to be on the light side. This is primarily because there are only 4 problem sets. Individually, the problem sets can take some time but if you start them early, they aren't a problem. The final exam (the only exam) was harder than the problem sets but in the same vein since it was open book and had unlimited time. Sometimes questions were unclear but the instructor and TA are happy to clarify problems, including problems on the exam.
This is truly an introductory course and assumes only fairly basic physics knowledge and little to no prior knowledge about space systems and remote sensing. The class is a very interesting survey of many different remote sensing methods and their applications. The workload is fairly moderate and is well spaced out throughout the quarter.
Interesting course, and Dr. Rosen is a really nice professor! We also had guest lectures from other JPL scientists, which was neat because we got to learn about a lot of different remote sensing topics.
Great course overall with appropriate pacing and assignments to solidify understanding. Field experts that did guest lectures added enormous value to the course content and made its applications very clear and interesting.
