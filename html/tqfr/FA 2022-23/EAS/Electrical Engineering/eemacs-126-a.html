Comments

Do you have any constructive comments for other students considering taking this course? In particular, comments about workload/distribution of the workload of the course, the necessity of the textbook, unexpected time requirements or flexibility, or unspecified prerequisites could be particularly helpful.

The course is extremely enjoyable and dives into the details of how the fundamentals of information theory came to be. The class focuses on the theory and proofs of theorems, while the homework sets would make you think on the concepts and may bring out any doubts. Prof. Effros is an amazing teacher, and the TAs do a great job in clarifying any queries (over email as well as during office hours). Homework sets are put together nicely and contain 5 questions and 1 bonus question. The textbook "Elements of Information Theory" is extremely relevant and should be read thoroughly. Very well structured course. Would highly recommend this course.
this is a good class, and you will get a lot out of lectures. this year we had to wear n95 masks to lecture (very mandatory) and I found myself still going to the majority of them! This was a surprise to me since I typically find any excuse to substitute lectures with the textbook. Homework problems are very reasonable.
The lectures were very nicely organized and Effros is an amazing lecturer who makes the concepts clear and interesting. The TAs were very helpful as well. The class is appropriately united at 9 units.

However, one of the issues with the class was that the homeworks were generally problems straight out of the textbook and most people did really well on them. However, the exams were essentially straight from lecture and were much harder than the homework. Thus, the homework don't give you the right sense of the difficulty of the exams.

This on its own would be totally fine, but another big issue is that not enough time was given for the exams. The midterm was only 90 minutes for an exam with 4-5 problems (almost a problem set) and difficulty much harder than the homework. Many people were in a rush to write down their solutions and even solved a problem fully but didn't have a chance to write it down, and thus lost points on it. This was a very stressful experience.

The exam being on Gradescope with a timer is also very stressful. They should just release the exam as a PDF (same as any other homework assignment), tell us the time limit for the exam at the top, and let us have our own time to submit it, rather than a timed exam on Gradescope with time to work on the exam and 10 minutes to submit.

The average on the midterm was 50-60%. The exams should be changed to be at least 3 hours for 4-5 problems, since 90 minutes tests how fast you can write more than it does the content. They have not released the final yet, but I hope that they increase the time for it. These policies makes it feel more like they are trying to create a distribution of grades for the course.

Overall, the course would be completely great if the exams had 3 hours for 4-5 questions instead of 90 minutes and were released to as any assignment would, where the time limit is given at the top, rather than being timed on Gradescope. Many classes I have been in at Caltech give the exams out as PDFs to us, and the Honor Code is effective so I don't think there are any issues with this. The exam policies makes the class very stressful, when it would otherwise be a fully enjoyable experience, since the other aspects of the course are really good.

The class is still worth taking if you're interested in Information Theory though, cause Effros is a really good lecturer and caring person. Just be prepared for a stressful time in the exams.
The textbook for this course is awesome! I found the midterm to be pretty time-constrained rather than understanding-contrained, so if you take this course, study accordingly :)

Then the sets got a little bit harder and final is hard, but there's a great lecture that's pretty mandatory (I guess the textbook is sufficient but the lecture teaches so much more intuitively and there's no lecture notes).
The class is very well-taught. Although it assumes no prior knowledge of information theory, the content is difficult and moves fast, so having taken EE160 before was helpful. The lectures are clear and thorough, and you should definitely go to them (there are no lecture notes). The homework is reasonable and doesn't take too long, but the exams are very difficult.
Textbook is definitely necessary for the course.
The course is very reasonably paced. Homeworks and exams are well-written. Prof. Effros gives some of the best lectures I've ever had. (I'm a PhD student.) On average, I spent about 5-7 hours per week on homework and textbook readings.
Although we are assigned to read through 2/3 of the textbook chapters, I personally find no problem with homeworks or exams without those readings. It is necessary to have previous exposure to discrete probability.
There is some "required reading" from the textbook. I found that doing the reading was not necessary for understanding lectures and I could just search the book for the information I needed for homework, but it was important to take thorough notes on the textbook material to be prepared for exam questions.
