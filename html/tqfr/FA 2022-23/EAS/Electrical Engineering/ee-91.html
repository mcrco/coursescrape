Comments

Do you have any constructive comments for other students considering taking this course? In particular, comments about workload/distribution of the workload of the course, the necessity of the textbook, unexpected time requirements or flexibility, or unspecified prerequisites could be particularly helpful.

Pick an achievable project. Remember, the most important thing is to turn an idea into a real prototype in under 10 weeks. So spend plenty of time deciding on something that is fun, may already exist in some online DIY space, and is relatively inexpensive (you're going to be paying out of pocket for all your parts).

If you are choosing between a new and powerful component compared to an old but reliable one, go with the latter. Having good documentation and plenty of reviews spares you unexpected disappointment and hours debugging, stressing, and then going back to square one.
This class is way too rushed. You are expected to come up with an idea, simulate it, prototype it, test it, manufacture and fabricate it based on errors in the prototype, and then make a final presentation. You have to make three reports - preliminary design review, critical design review, final report. All in one term. As such, I would recommend choosing an easier project so that each of these steps take less time. The workload is not evenly distributed, so you might want to try and get ahead if you have time.

The "lecture" is just a meeting with all the groups where you go over progress. I wouldn't call it useless but it isn't that helpful either (it's mandatory, though).

The content from EE 90 is particularly useful for this course, I would review it if you want. Ohanian will often send out resources (books, tools, articles). I think it is impossible to review all of it in a timely manner but I would save all the links, and try to review some of the stuff relevant to your project.

This is a senior design project class - you get out of it what you put into it. It's a great opportunity to learn a new software or learn about a field that you don't know much about.
This course is (obviously) really hard. Depending on your chosen project, even a very good TA will probably not be able to help you much, because there are just too many possible project topics and your TA probably will not have experience w/a couple EE subfields. Start your work early, don't procrastinate early in the course, and expect that your project probably won't work. Working as partners is really helpful because you can hold each other accountable/force each other to go into lab. The syllabus/requirements for project checkpoints is not super clear, err on the side of asking the TA too many questions about what they want to see in the submissions.
While I did learn a lot, this class is very disorganized and time-consuming. Richard sends out expectations for what to submit in assignments only a few hours before the deadline via email. Sometimes these emails even came after we'd already submitted assignments.

There is a lot of material crammed in a 10-week time. To compensate, you can try to pick an easier project that is attainable in 10 weeks, but you'll be told it's not interesting enough. Also, students have to pay for everything (components, PCBs, etc.) which is completely unfair and not standard in other departments. I dropped $300 on this class. Please, EE department, give us money to pay for these things if you actually want people to pick this major.

Each week there is a one hour lecture, but this "lecture" is just each group presenting their updates. It frequently went over time by 30+ min.
The class was a ton of work, as expected. I would definitely recommend thinking about your project a lot early on, maybe even before term starts. This term, all projects were done in groups of two, so you could find a partner and start brainstorming ideas before term. You can do pretty much any EE-related projects that you want, as long as you pick something complex enough that you can make two PCBs for (each partner makes one); although, also make sure that it can be reasonably completed in one term. Most of the time spent in this course was the in the latter half of the course, from CDR onwards, so it would be useful to spend more time towards the beginning to save yourselves time in the latter half (e.g. think more about the system design and making sure all components work together before buying everything to save yourself multiple Digikey purchases/shipping costs).

Overall, you'll get as much out of the course as you put in, and if you do have the time, it's really rewarding if you are able to make something cool that you're really interested in.
This class is a project class, and the time commitment may vary (there will likely be a week after you send your PCB to the fab house where you won't have as much to do), but you should not mistake that for a flexible commitment. Be diligent about scheduling meetings with your partner/group and making/keeping to a schedule. You will get to the end of the term sooner than you expect, and debugging your project in the lab will likely take a lot of time.

I would advise picking a project in an area where you understand the underlying theory well. As an example, my group did an RF project despite none of us having taken an RF class, and this was not necessarily the best idea because we spent too much time hashing out the theory (and we also made design mistakes in this regard that required another PCB revision). With that said, you should not make your project too simple -- you will not learn if you are not applying your prior knowledge to a project of sufficient scale/complexity. In other words, you get out what you put in with this class.

YMMV in terms of how helpful the prof and TA are in questions of design/debugging. Ohanian has robust RF background so he was helpful to us in that regard. It may be worth considering the strengths of the TA/prof when choosing your project; although some problems will stump even people experienced in that area, you're less likely to be stuck with something the prof/TA can't solve if it's an area they know well.

Designing things right the first time is much easier than debugging and making another revision. Read datasheets super closely - the smallest details are really important. I recommend paying attention to other groups' presentations, as you can sometimes get ideas for things in your own design that can be improved, as well as common pitfalls to avoid.

Picking something you're excited about will help as well. If you want a lot of out of this class, you will probably end up putting a lot of work in. Good luck, and I hope you make something you think is cool! If you don't care as much and see this class as a necessary evil, just make sure your partner also sees it that way.
The purpose of this class is to design and fabricate an entire PCB-based electronic system. Students work in pairs, and each student must design at least one PCB.

The design is completed in steps assigned to each week:
- project proposal
- critical component selection/preliminary design review
- circuit simulations/design analysis
- breadboarding/prototyping
- design freeze (ideally)/critical design review
- schematic and BOM
- parts placement
- completed layout and fabrication
- assembly and packaging plane
- test
- final report/demo

This is a very large amount of work and, due to the nature of electronic design, it's heavily backloaded. For this reason, it's important to stay on top of things from the start and try to get ahead as much as possible. You may even want to work before the start of the term, at least on choosing a project.

We had 1-2 hour class meetings every week during which groups provided updates on their progress. This acted as a strange blend between design review (with Richard) and quick summary, which I thought was largely a waste of time for everyone involved, even though it was interesting to see what other people were working on.

We also had weekly check-in meetings with the TA like in EE 90.

Be careful in choosing a project. Things that sound really straightforward on paper often turn out to have complications. Also think fully through your design in the early stages. The sooner you can catch mistakes or make design revisions, the less time it takes to handle them. In the breadboarding phase (if possible), make sure to validate your design to the fullest extent possible, because having a circuit you are confident in will help a lot when it comes to debugging the assembled board (or will allow everything to work first try).

Also, ask lots of questions, both technical and about the requirements of each course milestone. Although the results of the class are up to you, the class itself is managed rather poorly with unclear expectations, surprise deadlines, rubrics that aren't used, and/or grades that don't have written feedback for certain assignments.
As expected, this course is a lot of work, and the workload varies dramatically over the course of the term. You're on an extremely tight schedule, especially considering shipping times for parts and PCBs. This class should really be 2 or 3 terms.

Ohanian does as much as he can to help students, which is sometimes less than perfect since the projects are usually very advanced and can cover a wide range of fields in EE. Most of his help consisted of sending us lists of links for online articles/papers that might be helpful. He also helped us debug our boards in the lab when we got stuck a couple times.

There's a lot of flexibility in choosing your project and making design decisions, including flexibility in the course schedule/deadlines, as long as you can justify yourself. The grading is similar to EE 90, where it seems like as long as you put in effort and do good engineering you'll get an A, even if your project doesn't end up working perfectly.
EE 91 was a super rewarding but time-consuming course. I am really proud of my final project (even though it didn't end up working completely) and it is surprising how much you can get done in one term with the guidance of a super knowledgable and caring professor and TA. The grading is a bit unclear, but as long as you show a lot of effort on the deliverables, you will probably be ok.
