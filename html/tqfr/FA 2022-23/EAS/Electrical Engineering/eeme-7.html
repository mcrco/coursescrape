Do you have any constructive comments for other students considering taking this course? In particular, comments about workload/distribution of the workload of the course, the necessity of the textbook, unexpected time requirements or flexibility, or unspecified prerequisites could be particularly helpful.

This course is somewhat under united. The class itself assumes a lot of preliminary knowledge (circuits, breadboarding, fundamental E and M concepts from physics) for an introductory class. The labs can be quite jank and difficult at times, and lecture can be confusing if you don't have any prior experience. However, the labs do help you understand and learn a lot. Take this class if you're interested, and be aware that it may be a lot more work than expected.
This class is very time consuming! If you don't really have prior experience with circuits or EE then you will majorly struggle, although you will learn a lot. I think I spent 10-15 hours a week on this class, which is way too much for a 6 unit class. It was soul crushing at times in the sub basement of Moore. I wouldn't suggest taking as a first term frosh unless you want to be an EE.
I'd recommend to not take so much time on labs and just try your best and work with what you manage to get. If you explain why something might not work you will be fine because you can end up spending the entire day in lab if do not cut some losses. You won't fail and Glen and the TAs will give really good feedback on the report once you get it back and in class where you can ask questions.
This class is a lot of work and if you aren't engaged by the material you probably won't have a fun time. That being said: if you are interested in EE, or the connection between ME and EE on a low level, this is an extremely rewarding class. You get to build and understand increasingly complex circuits in the labs (after lab 2, there are 2 labs per week). This cumulates to a final project, where you get to design a working mechatronic system (for us, it was a line-following car).

For the lectures: You should go because Glen will usually hand back and review previous labs there. He will also go over the next labs, sometimes revealing hints that aren't in the lab writeup.

For the labs: If you aren't super experienced with circuits and motors, office hours are essential.
The TAs are very helpful for debugging and clarifying concepts, but they can't always save you. Sometimes isn't anything obviously wrong with your setup and the TAs say "Umm well just keep trying ¯\_(ツ)_/¯", and you have to spend a lot of time troubleshooting by yourself. If you're still stuck and the TAs weren't able to help, get Glen to look at your lab. Or: just piff and not finish the lab — it's possible to get a 70+ even if you are missing some parts.

For the final project: Just try something. Most people's projects don't fully work. Failing the final project will not make you fail the class.

Don't be discouraged if your circuits keep breaking or if you can't complete a lab. You are not stupid; this class is very difficult, especially for a frosh lab. Despite your grades on labs or how well your final project works, if you put in the effort, you will learn a lot in this class.

My time breakdown for each lab: https://imgur.com/a/4ysy4wH
If you are really interested in EE or have background, or are willing and able to put the time and effort into learning all of the EE material for this course, I would recommend it. You do a ton of circuitry stuff in the labs. That being said, the labs, at least for me, always took way longer than expected. Given that we had two per week, a bulk of my week was dedicated to this class and going through the labs and write-ups. You really do need to go to Office Hours, I found it one of the only ways to get through these labs since the TAs are pretty great. Honestly, figuring out how to get your circuitry to work/debugging it/writing up the reports takes a long time, so that's why I spent so much time in this class.

However, Glen George is a pretty amazing professor, and he is very forgiving when it comes to extensions on the labs and what not. Ultimately, the class was very interesting and I'm glad that I got all of the EE/circuitry background that I did and that I got to know my fellow frosh.
