Comments

Do you have any constructive comments for other students considering taking this course? In particular, comments about workload/distribution of the workload of the course, the necessity of the textbook, unexpected time requirements or flexibility, or unspecified prerequisites could be particularly helpful.

One of my favorite classes. Al is a fountain of knowledge. It's fun to come to class and learn about graphics in general and fun applied math stuff. Worth noting that homework takes time and the lectures aren't designed to help with it (it's all posted online, you could do the course right now). However, the TA's this year were great for helping with homework. Take it for fun if you have the time.
Interesting topics but if your linear algebra and C/C++ skills are not up to snuff this class is going be quite the challenging ride. Start assignments early and take advantage of OH, as the TAs are great at helping you debug and figure out how to navigate some of the more obtuse parts of the assignments (especially the shader assignment which is really vague compared to everything else). The supplementary lecture notes for each assignment will be very helpful to finishing the assignments as well so take care to read through those.
The material is fun and the assignments are so rewarding to do. You can really appreciate the immense amounts of love and labor that the TAs of this course put into refining the assignments and materials over the years.

It can take a while especially if you're not familiar with C++, and debugging can be hell, but the TAs are helpful and responsive and make it all go so much faster. Also the recitations are really important as they cover common pitfalls that can eat up hours of your time if you aren't aware of them!

The lectures are fun and Al is a really cool guy with a lot of wisdom to share. I highly recommend going to class and asking him questions just about anything because it's basically guaranteed he'll have something interesting to say about it.

It may be kind of hard to appreciate the perspective of the lectures in a high-pressure environment like Caltech, especially while the assignments for this class themselves are time-consuming and of course there are other classes as well. But personally I think it's good to make an effort to attend since Al's approach to graphics exemplifies the kind of thinking that I think makes Caltech so special.
If you know C, you'll be fine, even if you don't know C++.

The OpenGL version required by the class is ancient—debugging is very difficult as a result, because the resources on the internet have been long since updated for newer versions.

The class notes and assignment instructions themselves are of relatively poor quality. There are a fair number of typos; this wouldn't be a big deal, but they sometimes change the meaning of important sentences or equations. Even without the typos, the instructions are pretty ambiguous at times and require some guesswork to find the right interpretation.

Lecture attendance was unnecessary—to my understanding, they did not cover material directly related to the assignments, instead being largely tangential.

The assignments were very implementation-heavy, and didn't require much understanding of the theory behind the material. So, you get out what you put in from this class: If you want to read through the notes and understand the derivations, go for it; but most of the time, you won't need to if you don't want to.

The TA's tried their best to help us, but this was hampered by the fact that—as far as I could tell—they didn't have access to any reference solutions. Instead, it seemed that they had to rely on their own personal code, presumably from when they had taken the class themselves. One TA did have access to a previous year's Piazza board, which was sometimes helpful with debugging weird OpenGL issues. It felt like the TA's had very little official support from the professor, and instead had to try to help us in whatever ad hoc ways they could.

Some of the reference code provided for the later assignments wouldn't even compile or run on the course VM itself. Even worse, the reference images—which was the only way to verify if our outputs were correct—were sometimes completely inaccurate.

The whole class feels like something that was constructed 10 years ago and forgotten ever since.
