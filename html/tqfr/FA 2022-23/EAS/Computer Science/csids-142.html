This course is poorly ran and taught. It is the last week of classes and none of the sets are graded. The questions given on HW, quizzes, and tests are ambiguous and poorly written. The content is mediocre at best and sometimes not even related to distributed computing at all. Try to take one of the other classes for the advanced CS elective if possible.
This class is perfect for two types of people - those who want a relatively stress-free class to fulfill the advanced CS requirement (instead of taking 124, 137, 150a, 151, etc.), and those who want a rewarding class with very interesting algorithms and topics about distributed systems.

I took this class initially with the intention of exploring a new field, but mainly to fulfill my advanced CS requirement, and I ended up learning a lot more material that I found a lot more interesting than all of my other classes. I would 100% recommend this class if you would want to learn about distributed systems or maybe try out a new field in a stress-free learning environment.
Great class! The HW are interesting and minimal. The course notes are phenomenal, better than most things you will find about distributed systems online.

The course material is really fascinating and relevant to real world systems. It gives a great overview into how to reason about correctness of distributed algorithms, which is very different than reasoning about sequential ones.

The professor is amazing as well. There is a great effort into presenting the material in an interesting, gradual, and engaging way.
Course content was interesting. Great intro to crypto and models of distributed systems and distributed algorithms. Assignments were good overall, but problem statements often had errors. Course staff rectified errors, but it was somewhat inconvenient. Prof Chandy is not teaching the class next year I think, so perhaps a different experience can be expected in the future.
I really enjoyed the fact that the course had been modified to place more emphasis on more modern topics such as cryptocurrency and blockchain technology. Getting to learn some of the theory behind that in the context of distributed systems was really interesting, and definitely helped motivate taking the course.

Sets were engaging although inconsistent. One set took us around 30 minutes to figure out and write down a coherent proof for, whereas another took the better part of 6 hours to do, excluding time spent in office hours.

The midterm and final were pretty chill. Definitely worth taking and had some interesting material.
6 sets, 1 midterm, 1 final. Each set/exam comprised of mainly multiple choice questions in addition to a few proofs. Course website is very helpful supplement to lecture notes.
The lecture material was taught very well by Professor Chandy. He made lecture more interactive by asking questions throughout lecture to help our understanding and always made sure to be open for questions. He went through material at a steady pace and was always mindful of the other work we had. Overall, great class to take for advanced CS.
It was cool to take Professor Chandy's class on material that he developed while doing research. Even though he isn't teaching it anymore, I would highly recommend taking it. It isn't too much work, the material is interesting, and if you read the lecture notes, you can pretty thoroughly understand the content.
How do you design a fault-tolerant system? This class doesn't focus on questions that are this practical. In this class, the topics are highly theoretical. You learn about some interesting ideas relating to synchronizing a distributed state like the global snapshot algorithm, and it's cool that the professor was the one who originally proved some of these things, so that lets him explain it well. However, some topics stayed too theoretical for my taste. For example, when learning about the Byzantine algorithm, the discussion stays in terms of generals and lieutenants, but it's a real algorithm with uses in computer science. After this class, I still don't really know what that use is. I think the discussion of bitcoin was really interesting. The class is all informal proofs so the assignments are pretty straightforward (nothing super involved). Classes are mostly discussion based and engaging yet slow. Good light class to take, but didn't get too much out of it.
If you are debating which class to take as your advanced computer science class for the CS major, this is a good choice. This class is likely going to change a bit due to a swap in professors but in my experience, the material was taught very well, with very detailed lecture notes available. The sets did feel a bit strange though they were ultimately rewarding.
Chandy is a really great lecturer! He has lots of enthusiasm and cares about student understanding. But unfortunately he won't be teaching anymore, so I don't know if the class will be offered or who will be teaching it now.

This year the class was changed so that it was more of a broader overview of distributed computing and not as proof heavy as before. I personally liked this organization of the class. I liked the overview of Bitcoin and temporal logic during the second half of the class more than the first half, but that's just my opinion. The first half was more "proofy" than the second half and thus more challenging but still was taught well. The sets consisted of multiple choice quizzes with about 10 questions plus 1 or 2 proof style written questions. The same was true for the midterm and final.

The sets and exams were definitely easy compared to CS 38 and CS 21 but they were challenging at times, but nothing too crazy. The final was harder than the midterm.

I felt like the exams were weighted too much compared to the sets. The grading was 50% sets, 25% midterm, 25% final when I think a better breakdown is 60% sets, 20% midterm, 20% final like ACM 104.

Overall, I would definitely recommend taking this class if it is offered next time because it is a good intro to distributed computing concepts and Bitcoin!

I am not very interested in distributed computing, but I am still glad I took this class with Chandy. This was also his last time teaching the class, though, so future years may be different. Overall, a good option to satisfy the hard CS course requirement.

This is the single greatest class I have taken at Caltech. I really really hope it is continued in spite of Professor Chandy's retirement because it has broadened my horizons and shown me a whole different side of CS.

Professor Chandy has been an incredible lecturer. Throughout, I felt like he truly cared about our understanding. He was also very funny and made lectures very fun and easy to focus on. He would have takeaway slides where he would summarize the main ideas that he'd "want us to remember 10-15 years later".

The homework was designed to not waste your time at all. And if you attend every lecture, approaching the homework was not difficult. The course website has detailed notes that explain every concept very well. Still, I found lectures to be the best place to learn.

The class was very well organized and very accommodating. We had multiple weeks to do the final and the midterm. The midterm deadline was extended when many questions about the midterm were coming in. Every question was answered well.

If you do your part (attending lectures, and trying to understand concepts) this class is incredibly rewarding and very fundamental.

I REALLY hope this class is continued.
