Do you have any constructive comments for other students considering taking this course? In particular, comments about workload/distribution of the workload of the course, the necessity of the textbook, unexpected time requirements or flexibility, or unspecified prerequisites could be particularly helpful.

Workload is reasonable. All assignments are out of the book, which is accessible electronically.
The textbook is helpful.
I can say the workload is not overwhelming, however you might be better off referring to the textbook rather than to lectures if you really want to grasp the knowledge on thermodynamics. The textbook gives a more detailed explanation on every topic and it relates every topic to real world applications. Regarding lecture, It is important that you attend because a lot of the time I found myself understanding concepts better through the lecture notes.
I think that the textbook is not super helpful in this iteration of the class. Occasionally you can find example problems but I think that going to lecture will help you more with learning the content and doing the homework. Sometimes useful information about certain problems would be sent in an email or canvas announcement which wasn't always obvious, so keep an eye out for that.
If you attend office hours and maintain a good time management schedule, the course is well unitted. Perhaps try to utilize the textbook on top of lecture as well, might aid in understanding of how to approach concepts.
The notes are written out in a helpful module fashion where we fill in blanks with concepts, graphs, and important equations during class. The conciseness of notes organization is great. Make sure to ask lots of questions as well so you are clear about your understanding for approaching problems. The head TA Adrian is also amazing, so if he's still TAing go to his office hours too. Prof. Minnich is also great about coming around to students during class and if students have questions. I wish there were more extra credit or curves applied for the class though. I put in a lot of effort and time and will probably only get by with a B.
Overall I thought this was a pretty good course.

The grade breakdown was 45% for homework (lowest set dropped), 30% final, and 35% midterm. The course textbook was Fundamentals of Thermodynamics, Borgnakke & Sonntag, 10th edition.

For the midterm, we were told that it would take about 90 minutes (but we were given unlimited time in a single sitting) but most people spent several hours on it. The average was in the low 70s for the midterm but the professor did mention the course would get curved at the end. Personally, I did well on it but I spent a lot of time on the test.

The lectures are given with a good mixture of proofy explanations and example problems. Austin walks around the room to help students as they attempt to work problems out by themselves. The policy of no extensions on HW was weird and towards the end of the course the professor would randomly give everyone 1-2 day extensions (but would notify of the extension only a few hours before the sets were originally due)

As far as TAs, Adrian always did a good job crash-coursing the content for the problem sets the day they were due. I do wish that this session could have been earlier in the week to allow time to absorb everything and finish the sets.

Overall, it was a pretty good course and I found it much more manageable than ME12.
Me 11a taught by Prof. Minnich is a great class. The content is interesting and he explains why its important to learn. In addition, the pace he teaches the course content is actually digestible and the sets were fair in regards to length and difficulty (unlike 99% of classes at Caltech). Me 11a is the first STEM class I've taken at Caltech where the prof. doesn't make you feel like stupid for asking questions and puts in considerable effort into actually making sure you understand ~his~ answers to your questions (very rare for a prof. in my experience). Overall, it was great class that I feel like I learned a lot in. The midterm was just a bit scuffed. Make sure you know what an electric resistance heater is because you're not going to be taught it in class but 40% of your midterm grade is going to rely on it lol.

The lectures do cover the material well, but are rather unengaging (especially compared to ME12a). All in all, it's a fine course and fairly bearable as long as you review all the material from lecture. The weakest point in my opinion is the fact that although the prof teaches you the content of questions, he doesn't teach how to think critically about them very much. TA sessions greatly assist in supplementing this deficiency.

Most MechEs who take this class along with ME12a always feel like one or the other is more of their speed. As someone who was more comfortable with ME12a, I thought the class was still very cool and the conceptual questions occasionally asked in sets really helped some basic thermo ideas. Definitely takes a lot of time to understand, but really trying to understand the content as much as you can is very rewarding. Theres also no real need to know anything before going to the class. And I think its cool to just see and think about things in your everyday life through a thermo-like view.
