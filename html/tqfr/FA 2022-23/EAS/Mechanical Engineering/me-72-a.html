Comments

Do you have any constructive comments for other students considering taking this course? In particular, comments about workload/distribution of the workload of the course, the necessity of the textbook, unexpected time requirements or flexibility, or unspecified prerequisites could be particularly helpful.

You know what you're getting yourself into if you take this, I don't think my TQFR will change anything about it.
Ask anyone who has taken ME 72 about it and they will probably say something along the lines of, "Really fun, but a ton of work." I certainly heard that sentiment echoed many times before embarking on the adventure myself, but I definitely agree. For many people, myself included, it is the cornerstone of the MechE major. It is the class that everything builds up to. All of your theoretical classes sophomore year, as well as the experimental ones, all feed into ME 72. A large portion of the material presented in the lower level courses is useful in one way or another. For a lot of people, reading the TQFRs isn't going to change whether they are going to take it. Either you want to or you don't. But if you do decide to take it, best to know what you are getting into. Simply put, it's a lot. A lot of preparation, a lot of time in the shop, and a lot of work (especially when you take into account other classes). My advice is get a good team together, people you get along well with and that you are willing to spend a large amount of time collaborating with. Put in the work early. Deadlines can really sneak up on you. But all of that being said, it's a great class. Mello is awesome, Paul and Trent are amazing resources, and I'm really looking forward for what next term has in store.
ME 72a is actually fairly unit'd in my opinion. If you organize your time well and plan extensively, there's really no reason to be spending absurd amounts of time in the shop. In other words, the class is 9 units unless you choose to make it more.
The course is pretty heavily backloaded, once we started building my time commitment immediately went from around 6 hours a week to 20 and kept climbing from there, pretty much requiring me to put the rest of my classes on hold until after the mobility demo was over. It's easy to change your ideas and designs a bunch during the initial phase but you'll want to have things finalized as much as you can by CDR so you can get building right away and so you don't waste any time or money on things that don't contribute to the final product. For team selection it's important to have a group that can get along well and work out disagreements even when stressed and you'll benefit from having teammates with a variety of skills and backgrounds.
Was in the shop testing a mechanism and Mello asked me "Are you having fun? Because all I want is for you guys to all be having fun, you know, that's all I really care about."
