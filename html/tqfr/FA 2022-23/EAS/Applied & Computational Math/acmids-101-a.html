Do you have any constructive comments for other students considering taking this course? In particular, comments about workload/distribution of the workload of the course, the necessity of the textbook, unexpected time requirements or flexibility, or unspecified prerequisites could be particularly helpful.

I 100% recommend you take this course. Bruno is an excellent profesor and his enthusiasm for the topics is contagious.

For the problem sets only one question counts towards your grade and I liked this as it took pressure off and it enabled to enjoy solving the problems more. I'd say time spent on problems sets was also reasonable for a 12 unit course.

The TA's Daniel and Sebastian are also fantastic!!! Were super helpful in aiding my understanding of the content and helping with problem sets!
There isn't really a textbook and Oscar's notes are more than enough to handle what is going on. It requires a little bit of familiarity with complex analysis, but the most relevant theorems and formulas are covered in the first week of class. The grading policy is that your grade on each problem set is the highest individual score of any question which means you only have to submit one question and this makes it a little easier to handle the material. The first quarter was also basically no coding.
Although this was a 12-unit course, the workload was more than manageable since only the highest-scored homework question counts towards the homework grade. This allows for a good level of flexibility in the amount of work you'd want to put into the class. Oscar gives very good lectures with succinct explanations and examples for each concept. He boils down the essential ideas of each topic and presents them in an approachable and pedagogically-friendly manner as opposed to the way the main textbook presents it. These, in conjunction with his astounding enthusiasm, make for a very pleasant lecture experience. That said, it would definitely be quite important for one to have some preliminary background in analysis and ODE before taking this class since many concepts taught are built upon these undergraduate-level ideas.
I took this class as an advanced physics elective, and it was great! Professor Bruno is a very animated and engaging lecturer and he explains even the most complicated concepts very clearly, gives helpful examples of applications, is willing to review even content covered by other classes, and is great at answering questions. And I've definitely learned a lot of things that I think will be useful to me in the future. Also, because the grading policy is 100% homework and on each set your maximum score on a single problem is your score on the entire set, the class isn't even stressful. The only downsides are that the lecture notes that Professor Bruno posts aren't amazing, so lecture is definitely something you almost have to attend, and the TAs this year have an unfortunate tendency of writing down the solution on the board instead of answering individual students' specific questions.
Professor Bruno is the best lecturer I have ever had, and this is one of the best classes I've taken. Professor Bruno avoids skipping steps in lecture, explains everything thoroughly, focuses on developing intuition, and is a very engaging lecturer. I would strongly encourage anyone considering it to take it. The sets can be hard, but the grading policy is very generous (only your highest scoring problem on a set counts).
This course is challenging. But it will be fun if you are interested in learning asymptotic methods, which have plenty of applications for science and engineering.
This class was unlike any other ACM class I have ever experienced at Caltech. Oscar Bruno's unique lecture style involves really engaging the class, providing a plethora of examples to follow along and explore applications to engineering, optical physics, and more.

The textbook is useful as well, with some of its examples containing explanations essential to fully understanding the sets. Overall, I loved the fact that this class was entirely based on sets because it gave me more time to really go over the concepts covered. Initially coming from a pure math background, I did not expect to develop a love for asymptotic approximations or perturbation theory but now I think I may consider pursuing a research opportunity in this field. Furthermore, it was really nice that this class had a policy of letting us only be graded for the one best scoring problem per set because the problems certainly can be very involved (it is a 12 unit class, after all).

Definitely go to office hours if you can, even if it is just for clarification because it can save you a lot of time. I really enjoyed Sebastian's office hours because they were conducted in a recitation-like style.

Strongly recommend this class to anyone interested in approximation methods (basically every applied STEM field can find use in this).
