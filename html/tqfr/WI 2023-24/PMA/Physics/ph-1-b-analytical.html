Ph 1b-an starts with special relativity, then moves on to electrostatics. Ph 1b-prac is electromagnetism. The two tracks are quite distinct.
I really loved this class' content, but I am glad that it is pass/fail. Quizzes are 40% of the grade, and TAs are really strict with grading. Even though I really want to take 1c anal, I am scared of taking it on grades.
Ph 1b (analytical) is definitely a step-up from Ph 1a, but definitely worth it and rewarding. I thought that the special relativity portion of the course (the first month) was quite chill, but the E&M part was a bit harder. The vector calculus is pretty relaxed and surface-level, nothing crazy (regular calculus will have you prepared). The quizzes are a bit challenging, but nothing crazy. The analytical track for physics is definitely worth it if you have any interest in physics, and it teaches it in a much better and more exciting way than the practical track. Something to keep in mind is Ph1b and 1c are continuous, so if you choose the analytical track for 1b, you should (but not required) take analytical for 1c, which will be taken on grades.
Take the analytical track. The math is annoying sometimes, sure, but the understanding of special relativity and E&M that you get in the end is worth it. Instructor Patterson also makes learning the content very straightforward.

The course is manageable if you put your mind to it, and the workload is fairly light.

Course covers special relativity, culminating in 4-vectors and collisions at relativistic speeds. Course then makes a sudden shift into electrostatics, capacitance, ending with basic circuit analysis. Lecture is always excellent! The quizzes finals really don't hold back, jumping right into these multistep problems. But having done the sets makes them very approachable.

Two lectures per week (Tues,Thurs) and recitations on Wed & Thurs. 9 weekly problem sets with 4-5 problems each, 4 biweekly quizzes with 1-2 long problems each, and a four-hour final exam with lots of problems. Homework was due each Tuesday at midnight.

Textbooks used were Helliwell's "Special Relativity" ch.1-13, Purcell's "Electricity and Magnetism" 3rd edition ch.1-4, and Shey's "Div, Grad, Curl, & All That" as a supplemental optional resource.

Here is the weekly course calendar:
1) time dilation, length contraction, simultaneity, lorentz transform, velocities
2) relativistic mechanics, collisions, and decay
3) mass and energy, nuclear reactions, doppler effect, beaming
4) electric force/field/potential energy, gauss' law, E of various objects
5) electric potential, divergence & gauss' theorem
6) curl & stokes' theorem, i forgor
7) conductors, uniqueness theorem, poisson eq, laplace eq, image charges
8) capacitance, various capacitors, energy, series and parallel, current derivation, Ohm, power
9) dc circuits, kerchoff laws, cool demos
