What would you tell a student thinking about taking this course in the future?

TLDR: Ph 125b continues from Ph 125a by treating approximation methods, electromagnetic interactions, and scattering. The first third of this is a more challenging treatment of material from Ch 21ab, and everything else is entirely new. In theory this is chapters 16-19 of Shankar, but I think that Griffiths, Sakurai, and Atkins (MQM) are also helpful.

COURSE CONTENT: If you are taking this class after or instead of physical chemistry, note that the first third of the course will revisit the variation method, Ritz method, time independent (non-degenerate and degenerate) and time dependent perturbation theory, but at a more mathematically-challenging level than you will have seen in Ch 21ab. The most new material in Ph 125b is in the discussion of scattering, which takes up the second part of the course.

RESOURCES: Make sure you reread over the lecture notes after lecture - derivations in this class are sometimes multiple lectures long and if you don't review after lecture, you'll be completely lost the next class.

The official textbook for this class is Shankar, which is useful, and the unofficial textbook is Sakurai, which is also useful, especially for the approximation methods section. Griffiths is also surprisingly useful if you ever need to review calculations involving a central potential, and Griffiths also has a small discussion of electromagnetic interactions (which you should definitely read). For scattering, Shankar is fine but Sakurai is confusing - I found the discussions in other books a bit easier to follow (if you used it in chemistry, MQM by Atkins is helpful).

The initial treatment of scattering theory, in terms of wavepackets instead of momentum eigenfunctions, is not in any of the listed textbooks but can be found in this article: https://pubs.aip.org/aapt/ajp/article/33/1/20/1038316/Scattering-of-Wave-Packets

The YouTube lectures by Prof. Zweibach, for MIT OCW Quantum Physics III, are also incredibly helpful since the course closely follows Ph 125b's topics: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_OZXEb8FxZQ&list=PLUl4u3cNGP60Zcz8LnCDFI8RPqRhJbb4L

THOUGHTS: Overall, I found Ph 125b to be a chemically-relevant course that also introduced me to some very interesting topics in physics. A must-take for anyone interested in physical chemistry! However, if you're a chemistry major intending on taking this class (which you probably are; why else would you be reading this? :P), similar to Ph 125a, you will need to teach yourself about Green's functions, complex integration, etc. Also, note that if you choose to replace Ch 21a (which introduces variational method and nondegenerate perturbation theory) with Ph 125*a*, you will only get to these approximation methods in Ph 125*b*.
