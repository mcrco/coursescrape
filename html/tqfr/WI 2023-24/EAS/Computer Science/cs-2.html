What would you tell a student thinking about taking this course in the future?

The course is uniquely structured. Lectures are pretty interactive, and Prof. Blank encourages you to answer questions. Lectures are actually pretty helpful to understand the week's lab & project. There's a diagnostic at the end of every lecture on the material taught that day, and you're given a second attempt if you don't pass the first time. You enter a queue when you go to OH, and are given 8 minutes with a TA at a time before you have to rejoin the queue. You work with a partner in your section on each week's lab. Labs are decently manageable with a TA present to answer any questions. The course material is challenging and a sizeable step-up from CS 1. Expect to spend a few hours each week on difficult debugging. That being said, the material is interesting, and Prof. Blank teaches well. The weekly projects are challenging, but you don't have to complete every single task within the project to meet the pass line.
Take this class if you are VERY COMFORTABLE with java and is a CS major or minor required to take this. To all the MechE's, I wouldn't recommend taking this class too much. The in-lecture diagnostics were unhelpful at best: writing out code by hand in an environment that doesn't catch syntax errors or allow code testing is not how CS knowledge should be evaluated. Furthermore, having them being done in class takes away valuable lecture time for those that need it. It also doesn't help that the diagnostics are sometimes confusing or near incomprehensible or even wrong (i.e. "which data structure would you use to determine if a character is uppercase or lowercase?" should not have "Binary search tree" as its answer.) The diagnostics don't even meet their purpose of incentivizing people to come to lecture, as if one passes the ten required diagnostics, there is no longer any external incentive to come to lecture. At the same time, the second attempt for diagnostics are completable at home and still count for credit, and, as above, the diagnostics, sometimes, are, as this sentence is, incomprehensible. This is even ignoring that simple errors unrelated to lecture content will cause you to fail the entire diagnostic, with no partial credit. As such, your performance on the diagnostic is legitimately independent of lecture attendance and does not even serve its purpose of incentivizing students to come to lecture. Coming to lecture should be an independent decision of the students based on the quality of the lecture; we are people with agency and should not be forced to suffer through an hour better spent elsewhere.

On the topic of lectures, CS 2 was slightly better than CS 1, but also a lot of the same. The instructor was somewhat patronizing in lectures and I got the feeling they didn't believe that the students could interact with the abstract topics presented. A majority of the audience participation in lectures felt forced and uncomfortable, while the rest came from a select few people, which created what I felt to be a combative relationship between the instructor and students. If possible, the class environment should be noncombative, if not collaborative.

The grading policy for this course is, frankly, overengineered. There is no reason it should take nearly two pages to explain how to pass the class, or for floor or ceiling functions to be anywhere in the syllabus. As an example, I was required to get at least one "A", three "B"s, two "C"s, and one "D", as well as at least a "C" on the third, fifth, and seventh projects to pass the class. For several projects, it was impossible to even get a "D", and on the fifth project, it was impossible to get even a "C". When viewed from the perspective of a student that needs to complete these projects, these grading standards make little sense. Furthermore, the way that the projects were graded required students to do two to three times the work to earn an "A" on a project than would be needed to earn a "C" or "D". Thus, the project grading scheme seems to be centered more around how much work one is willing to dedicate to a specific project than a measure of the amount of content you know. The current grading scheme needs to be altered to be simpler, more representative of the course material, and less confusing.

Several labs in this course were either poorly designed or just not labs. The difficulty scaling on the labs was very inconsistent, and I spent anywhere ranging from 3 minutes to several hours completing the lab assignments. Furthermore, several labs contained no code, but text entry into fields on a page on the course website. On one occasion, the entire morning lab section was stuck for around 20 minutes attempting to solve one section of a lab, and it turned out the automatic grader only accepted the full, written out option listed next to its corresponding letter. This course requires more comprehensive guidance in general.

There were several classes I found very annoying, in specific the one where the entire class was spent doing the same diagnostic four times. Instructor Blank made the learning environment seem actively hostile on occasion, and in addition to the clear patronization, made lecture something I actively avoided except to do the diagnostic.

In full, dealing with the idiosyncrasies of instructor Blank and this course in general thoroughly drained my enthusiasm for learning computer science. As a chemistry option who wants to learn computer science because of its potential applications to my intended field, and also, more importantly, because it seems fun and interesting, I found this class to be something I had to force myself through and spend far more time than necessary on, even when I have studied all the course content already.
Expect to put in some time and effort, but you will get a lot out of it. CS 2 is honestly one of the most solid learning experiences I've ever had, and it was an interesting and rewarding class.
Even if you have experience with java programming, this course is still very valuable in terms of understanding the foundation of java datastructures. I believe that the takeaways from this class can be helpful in the future when designing more specialized programs.
