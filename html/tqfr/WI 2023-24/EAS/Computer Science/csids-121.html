CS 121 was (by a large margin) the most disorganized and poorly-executed class I have ever taken at Caltech. While the course content itself is fairly easy (relational algebra on the first few sets, then basically just brain teasers with SQL queries and DDL setup for the remainder of the sets - I think part of one of our sets was moved to the final and became extra credit) and there's no midterm, the organization and grading practices of the class are quite frustrating. Before discussing the variety of factors which made this class frustrating, I wanted to note that the course content itself felt quite incomplete, especially relative to databases classes taught at other schools. For example, database concurrency is not at all discussed in this class (which seems like an extremely important topic to me), nor is the concept of transaction processing (though it does implicitly come up when building the final project).

There were no lectures for the first couple of weeks, and by week 4, I had basically no idea when lectures actually were, since the professor would arbitrarily set SEVERAL different lecture times by vote on Discord the night before or morning of. The same happened with OH, making it difficult to get clarification on problem sets. The grading on the problem sets is extremely harsh as well - since this class has "reworks" (which you earn the right to do by completing lecture exit tickets), the graders appear to be instructed to grade harshly to encourage the use of a rework. The issue is that without knowing when lecture is actually occurring, it is impossible to do the exit tickets, and thus impossible to get more than the 2 reworks you initially begin with. This effectively makes your margin for error extremely low on the problem sets unless you camp on the Discord for updates from the professor and have the ability to drop everything at a moment's notice to attend CS 121 lecture.

Moving on to the Discord - while it was nice to have the professor and the TAs interacting with the students in a forum like Discord, there were many critical course updates which were communicated solely through the Discord in the form of a singular message, which often got lost within the slew of messages sent by students, as well as the other messages the professor themselves sends. This made it difficult to determine basic things such as the deadlines of assignments.

Finally, the process of receiving grades and feedback from course staff is incredibly slow. I am writing this TQFR over a week after the end of finals week, and I have still not received a final grade for this class - in fact, around half of my assignments are still ungraded as well. Getting questions answered by the professor was also pretty challenging, as I would not receive responses to basic questions for several days.

If you're interested in taking this class, you'll probably do alright as the content itself isn't very challenging, but be prepared to basically treat this class as a self-study, independent endeavor with limited support from course staff. Also, make sure to start the final project (an implementation of a database of your choice paired with a Python-based CLI or a Node.js webpage) a bit early (probably at least 5 days before the due date) - the amount of tasks which comprise the final project accumulate to a fairly substantial amount of work, and you might have to stay up late writing a lot of code if you put it off too much.

Even before then, the course gave me a lot of anxiety; El routinely sent essay-length Discord announcements and just made me question what was going on.

Assignments aren't bad and the course policy included the opportunity to rework them. Then, the final project specification was not finalized pretty much ever; it was a copy of last year's (with some parts that no longer applied to us) and said the deadline was "Friday +/- 1 day" which resulted in a lot of confusion.

As a note, most of the content is basic and could be learned from watching a YouTube video. You'll mostly just learn how to write SQL for a term (which, at least if you're a CS major, is really easy). Thankfully, you will learn about relational algebra and entity-relationship (ER) diagrams, but we didn't get to normal forms this year. The final project is decently interesting since you get to write a Python app to interact with a database you design, but that assignment came with its own challenges (as you can see above).

Overall, this course covers useful content, but it's not worth the headache; you can teach it to yourself in a day. El is accommodating and kind, but they seem to offer extra OH and lectures at the expense of other parts of their courses.

This course is disappointing to say the least. The topic is interesting and Professor Hovik clearly seems excited about it. Unfortunately, the quality of the course is sub par any course you could find online and complete in the span of 5 hours.

Starting off with lectures, they were close to non-existent. Professor Hovik really wanted students to attend lecture but they did not bring anything additional to the textbook. It really felt like wasted time and would discourage anyone from going to lecture. Once is understandable but multiple times made it frustrating.

The assignments were dull but did the job I guess. The final project was sort of interesting but very poorly distributed over the course of the term. Essentially, students would have to make proposals for projects without knowing the expectations nor the technical feasibility since the content was not covered and then were expected to complete it in the span of finals' week. The project alone required 30+ hours and the fact that some assignments took barely 2 hours made it extremely frustrating to have such a severe time constraint on the final project. Looking back on it, the poor organization of "content" and assignments (deadlines changed weekly and subject to whenever the assignments would get released) made it impossible to start the final project early even though multiple students asked on Discord.
