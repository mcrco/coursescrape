This course is a great way to get a hands on software engineering experience for the entirety of ten weeks - certainly, there don't seem to be too many courses at Caltech quite like this, and if this sounds exciting to you, absolutely take it.

With that said, make sure your team evenly distributes the workload, especially for the first few projects. I was really excited to take this course because of how exciting the projects seemed, but the workload within my team wasn't balanced, which often led to me being frustrated with how much I was putting in (especially around project 4), despite not having to take this course for any reason other than interest.

If you have a team that you know will evenly distribute work, this class will be super fun. If not, be prepared to take the risk. Even so, for the projects where I personally found myself disproportionately contributing, it didn't take *that* much time. Then again, this could vary depending on your comfort level with Python, your understanding of the intricacies of your code, and how "well-designed" your code is. I will say that if your code is designed relatively well from the start, future projects will be relatively easy.

All in all, we're expecting a good grade, but I wasn't happy about how work was distributed within my team. So make sure you know how much your teammates are willing to put in (and actually do put in) early in the course.

I personally think this course is great to take. Though it is a lot more work than you are probably used to, it was a great introduction to industry. I got to experience coding as they would in industry with writing own tests and stuff. It was coding in Python so the language part wasn't hard. I spent about >20 hours especially on the first and fourth/fifth project. I spent about 15 hours on the other two projects. The first project and fourth project take the most time since the first project is just starting and then the fourth project is making optimizations to your code so they past the performance tests. Since you create a spreadsheet engine, you implement a features every two weeks. So there are a total of 5 projects with each project due every 2 weeks. There are 4 late tokens which were really helpful since sometimes we found bugs the day off and had to take a late token.
I would say office hours given by Donnie was probably the best to attend. Donnie knew exactly what acceptance you failed when you asked and could give you test cases in which your engine would fail. We had a few bugs that we thought we fixed in the first week but it turns out we didn't so attending office hours helped.
