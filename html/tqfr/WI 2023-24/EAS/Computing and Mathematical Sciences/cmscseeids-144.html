What would you tell a student thinking about taking this course in the future?

I generally like this course and would recommend it for someone who needs to fulfill their "hard CS" requirement. However, my experience was the class is 9 units of content with 12+ units of work. So I definitely found myself more frustrated with the course organization/setup than I expected to be given its reputation.
Take it! Prof. Mazumdar is great. I thought it was interesting and saw relation to the real world. That being said, it is very mathy and proofy. We took a look at expected regret, auctions, pandemics, degree centrality, and web graphs etc. There were like two blog posts, about 6 homeworks, 2 competitions, and a project proposal. There were in person quizzes (not graded) in order to get late tokens. The projects and competitions are in groups. The TAs for the course were decent, but I'd make sure you have people to work with because OH are not easy to attend.
The subject matter of this course was very interesting especially given our collective experience with Covid-19 and the internet. Many of the concepts made a lot of sense to me and it was cool to learn about these ideas formally. I highly recommend getting the textbook because it was super accessible and a great addition to lectures. There were 6 homeworks that mainly consisted of proofs with some coding. There were also two projects: Pandemaniac (involved coding) and Pandemaniac++ (did not involve coding). These projects were fun but Pandemaniac was run poorly by the TAs and ended up pushing all assignments back a week. We also were assigned two "blog posts" which required us to write about anything involving networks. Additionally we had to write three project proposals to be used in future networks classes. These smaller assignments felt like busy work and I don't think students got out of them what the professor intended. For example, the blog posts were posted for the class and meant to be read, but I don't think many people actually read any of them. The grading for this class has been unreasonably slow. Overall, CS144 had very engaging and relevant material, but definitely had it's flaws (I believe the professor was new to teaching the course this year). I'd recommend taking this class to satisfy the "hard" CS requirement.

This is a great class to take! The sets are long and time-intensive but OH help a lot. Content is interesting and the course structure is established, and Eric was a good lecturer. There is a nice mix of theory, math, economics, and game theory in the course, which is a nice change from many of the very theory heavy CS courses. Sets have both coding and proof sections so you get a bit of both. Make sure you have a good team for the projects in the class, otherwise, they are quite hefty. Overall, definitely a 12 unit class but relatively fairly united and well organized -- worth taking!

Out of all the "harder" CS classes available, I definitely think this class is not only one of the better taught ones, but also one that really pushes you beyond the boundaries of what you studied so far. In general, I dont think CS majors at the stage of taking this class have necessarily had a lot of exposure to networks, and this class really delves into all their properties, mechanics, and sub-components through really interactive problem sets and (competitive) group projects. I definitely feel like I was actually genuinely interested in learning the material and doing the projects for this class, which I definitely cant say for all caltech classes.
