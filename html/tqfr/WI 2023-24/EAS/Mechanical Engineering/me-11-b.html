What would you tell a student thinking about taking this course in the future?

ME11b with Guillaume is simultaneously more and less chill than ME11a. You don't actually cover too much entirely "new" material--it feels more comfortable than 11a because you have some background in thermo this time around. That being said, the sets varied significantly in amount of time necessary to complete them; Blanquart will tell you up front they average out to have correct uniting, so be prepared for a few brutal weeks but then some more chill ones to balance it out. The class gets more theoretical, especially in the math side, but you cover some fun topics like combustion--I'd recommend reviewing chemistry concepts beforehand and finding some friends who are cracked at thermo already if you can :)

Me11b was a pretty interesting course, but definitely different than Me11a. Whilst Me11a was more of an introduction to the sort of fundamentals of thermodynamics, Me11b felt much more like a class that was aimed at introducing the ways that thermodynamics is used in a couple of different subfields. At least this year, we covered refrigeration and heating cycles, mixtures, thermodynamic relations, combustion, lots of other chemistry-related topics, and then radiation and conduction heat transfer. As you can imagine, you will have to recall a lot of the fundamentals that were introduced in Me11a in order to understand what is going on in this class. I also found myself needing to review chemistry and reactions for the combustion unit.

As for the style of the class, Professor Blanquart is a really great lecturer. He makes great effort to ensure that all of his students are learning and he really is the right professor to be teaching thermodynamics. The lecture notes are great and Professor Blanquart makes sure to involve the class during his lectures to make sure that you are engaged and actively participating. This was really helpful since at the end of the day Me11 is a conceptually difficult course. In addition to his lectures, Professor Blanquart also formulates the set questions in a way that you are challenged to think about the fundamentals of what you have learned. The questions were not simply following a procedure and plugging in numbers. They always required critical thinking and tested whether you actually understood the thermodynamics at its core. This, of course, is super helpful in making sure that you get the most out of the class.

Lastly, as I mentioned before, the main reason Me11 is a difficult class is because conceptually, the material you are learning is not easy. Lots of it is unfamiliar to most, and you might have to revisit some of it a couple of times before you fully grasp it. As such, my recommendation would be to make sure that you set aside time, in addition to the lectures and the homeworks, to sit down with the lecture notes and go over them again. It can be easy to just get the homework done each week and then not think about the material beyond that. To get the most out of Me11, take some time reviewing the slides and summarizing the content yourself. Come up with your own questions and ask them, and that will be very helpful in making sure that you actually walk out with what you need to know about thermodynamics.
