Me12b can seem a little bit different than what you might have encountered in Me12a, both in terms of work and material.

As for work, I found the amount of work to be a bit less than what we had in Me12a. Of course this could vary between years, but I found that I was able to complete the homeworks in a reasonable amount of time this quarter. Of course, I recommend starting the sets as early as possible so that in case you run into any issues, you can ask at office hours. For getting the homeworks done, the textbook will help a lot. Before starting the homework, I would always review the corresponding chapters in the book, look through their examples, and then start working on the sets. At the start, the textbook was a little less helpful since we had more conceptual and fundamental questions that were asked (like derivations and explaining conceptually certain things) but as the class went on, we got more of the type of questions where there is typically a procedure to follow, which was more like Me12a. Either way, the sets were still pretty reasonable in terms of how much time they took, and if you work through all of them, they really do help you actively learn the material. Also the TAs were super helpful for this class!!

As for the actual content, like I said, this might strike you differently than Me12a. In Me12b, we started off with bending of members, and then discussed beams under bending and how to analyze their shape. Then we went into shear stresses and a more detailed analysis of shear than in Me12a, before talking about torsional loadings and stresses. The class then moved on to the next big topic, which was stress and strain transformations, which is basically seeing how we can model the stresses and strains in different directions if we know the stresses and strains on a given element in a given orientation. Then the last major part of the class is discussing failure theories, failure due to buckling, and energy methods. At least for me, I really felt like these topics were ones that I could more directly seeing myself using in later courses and just general engineering practice. I think that the topics of Me12b are a very nice extension of where you leave off in Me12a, as now you are talking the basic ideas of stress/strains and different kinds of loadings, and seeing how these actually deform bodies in various ways. As such, I found the course content very interesting and it really has made me consider taking more mechanics of materials courses since it was so interesting.

Lastly, I would highly recommend reading the book to make sure that you absolutely understand the course material. It can be very easy to just look at examples and get done with the homeworks quickly, but what I found is that actually taking the time to learn the fundamental concepts by reading each chapter of the book was extremely useful for me actually internalizing the material. Also I found the midterm and final to require a bit more fundamental understanding and figuring out how to apply concepts rather than just doing a computation you have already done many times before.

All in all, the concepts of Me12b are very cool, the course has a reasonable workload, but make sure to take the effort to actually internalize the material.
