Comments

What would you tell a student thinking about taking this course in the future?

The class is listed as a 9 unit course but it go way above this estimation, which is apparent with most lab-based courses at Caltech. My group usually took 12-16 hours every week. The course goals are somewhat flexible. How the course goes is very dependent on who you decide to group up with and their schedules, so try to coordinate this before going into the course.
Gunter might just be the greatest professor of all time. I think this class has taught me more than any other class I've taken, just because it teaches you how things are actually made. This is also a very very very CS heavy course, so make sure you have at least one CS major or people who are affluent in numpy/general python in your group, 2 CS majors is probably ideal.
This course gives you an opportunity to completely design a robot arm from the ground up (apart from the choice of motor), which is a fairly unique opportunity compared to other schools from my experience. If you're interested in robotics, and have some time I *highly* recommend taking the course.

With that being said, as a senior applying to grad school and completing visits during Winter term, I really struggled with collaboration with my team. We were very fragmented, and our progress suffered because of it. If you are also a senior applying to grad school, make sure to give adequate time for your visits and interviews (emails and stress also take a surprising amount of time lol). I would still recommend taking this class, but choose a project with a dedicated fallback plan.
ME134, like most project courses, ends up being a large amount of work for a 9 unit class. However, it was one of my favorite courses at this school, where opportunities to get hands-on project experience in robotics are few and far-between. Gunter and the TAs go above and beyond to support each team's projects, making the experience meaningful and instructive, without anyone getting stuck on weird bugs for days on-end. If you're even remotely interested in robotics, I would recommend you take this class.

As for the course structure, there was a weekly (optional) lecture where Gunter and/or one of the TAs would cover material that was directly relevant to issues teams are facing in their assignments/projects. Since most of the relevant theory was covered in ME133a, this course jumps straight into working with hardware from week 1. In groups of 4, weeks 1-3 were devoted to getting different parts of a 3DOF arm working (motors, inverse kinematics, and then sensing). Weeks 3-10 are then devoted to a more in-depth project of each group's choice, always centered around a robotic arm but also involving other systems (cards, balls, etc...). There was a short report due each week to document progress made, but the bulk of the work was the actual project.
It is a really fun course that gives you the opportunity to apply your knowledge of robotics into a project you can be really proud of.
This class is a super fun application of the concepts from ME133a and ME133b (which can be taken concurrently). It is really rewarding to manage a codebase and add features and then see your robot act with those new features in real time. I would have spent 40hr/wk in lab on this class if I had the time, and I already spent more time than I should have on it.

Choose your group wisely, and try not to let the work division become too unequal. Make sure at least one member of your group attends lectures and communicates information to everyone else. Plan out when you'll be in lab - too many people in lab at once is not productive. The sweet spot is 1-3, with 3 pushing it. Set goals and adhere to them - don't let your timeline slide. Iron out precision early on -- it will be very annoying to deal with later. And have fun! This is an amazing class and Gunter has prepared some very detailed and comprehensive materials to help make using ROS and HEBI motors and USB cameras go as smoothly as possible. Take this class if you're interested and willing to put in ~9-12hr/wk.
Great course! Definitely recommend for anyone who wants to apply the lessons they learned in 133a
