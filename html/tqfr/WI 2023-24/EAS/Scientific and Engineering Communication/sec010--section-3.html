Comments

What would you tell a student thinking about taking this course in the future?

Well, you're probably taking this class because you're required to, but here we go anyways.

SEC 10 is a mandatory-attendance class taught at 9, 10, or 11 am (depending on your section) on MWF. You can miss at most three lectures before you fail the class, so definitely try to get into the 11 am section which is at an actually reasonable time. The lectures themselves are quite engaging and Prof. Gold knows his topics well. He does a good job breaking down an effective presentation into details that are possible to tackle, even if you're new to presenting.

The class seems to struggle a bit with pacing, given how many different kinds of students there are. You give four presentations over the course of the term, approximately one every two weeks:
- A one-minute personal story about your life, accompanied by a photo
- A two-minute elevator pitch to President Rosenbaum, intended to be about a scientific topic but also can be done about a topic related to improving Caltech
- A four-minute "chalk talk", where you explain a technical topic to a 9th grade audience using a whiteboard
- A ten-minute "seminar", where you explain a technical topic to peers (who aren't in your field) using a pre-prepared slide deck. The slides are also graded on their graphical design.

All of these are done without a script. This seems both a very fast pace of presentations for someone who's uncomfortable presenting, while simultaneously quite slow if you are comfortable with presenting. It's sort of an unhappy medium? The first two presentations are graded only on completion (although you still get feedback about how you did), while the last two also have grades based on how well you did.

The grading is incredibly kind and forgiving. You can do redos on all of the assignments until you get full credit, which is absolutely incredible. Prof. Gold gives detailed and immediate feedback, and is happy to meet to talk at any hour of the day or weekend.

There's hidden assignments in this class which aren't listed on the syllabus. In addition to just presenting, you also have to submit plans for what you're going to present and submit reflections after each presentation. Some presentations also have additional written assignments on top of those. These are fairly short but still a significant chunk of work. Some lectures are skipped as "homework days" to allow you to work on these, so I think the class is probably still fairly united, but it's a bit of a rough surprise nonetheless.

All of the slides are posted after class, and there isn't a textbook. Prof. Gold has a technical background himself in physics before he moved to working in presentations, so he's a good choice for teaching this class. On the whole, this class could've been a lot worse for a required presentation class, and I really appreciate how forgiving the grading and class structure is. I think Prof. Gold is doing his best with a requirement that's kind of difficult to make work, for everyone involved.
